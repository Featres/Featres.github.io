Chapter 6: Second-Order Cone Programs

Convex Optimization — Through the Lens of Algorithms and Applications Chapter 6

This chapter introduces Second-Order Cone Programs (SOCPs), one of the most important classes of conic optimization problems. SOCPs sit between linear programs and semidefinite programs in terms of expressive power: they can model everything an LP can, plus problems involving norms, quadratic objectives, and quadratic constraints. We begin by defining the second-order cone (Lorentz cone), establish its key structural properties (self-duality, properness), then build up to the SOCP formulation, its duality theory, and several important special cases including quadratic programs (QP) and quadratically constrained quadratic programs (QCQP). The chapter concludes with real-world applications such as portfolio optimization and computing distances between polyhedra.

6.1 The Second-Order Cone

Before we can talk about second-order cone programs, we need to understand the cone itself. The idea starts with a very general construction called a light cone.

Definition 6.1.1 — Light Cone

Let \(S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) be a convex set. The light cone generated by \(S\) is:

\[\{(x, c) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} : x \in c \cdot S\}\]

This is always a convex cone.

The intuition is simple: take a convex shape \(S\) (think of a disk or a ball), place it at height \(c = 1\), and then "project rays" from the origin through every point in \(S\). As \(c\) grows, the allowed region for \(x\) scales linearly — you get an ice-cream-cone shape. The most natural and important case is when \(S\) is the unit ball.

Definition 6.1.2 — Second-Order Cone (Lorentz Cone)

The second-order cone (also called the Lorentz cone) is the set:

\[\mathcal{Q}^n := \{(x, c) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} : \|x\|_2 \leq c\}\]
Intuition: The Ice-Cream Cone

Think of \(\mathcal{Q}^2\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\). A point \((x_1, x_2, c)\) is in the cone if \(\sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2} \leq c\). If you slice this cone at any fixed height \(c = h\), you get a disk of radius \(h\). As you go higher, the disk gets bigger — the whole shape looks exactly like an ice-cream cone with its tip at the origin. For \(\mathcal{Q}^1\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\), it is simply the region between the lines \(x = c\) and \(x = -c\) for \(c \geq 0\), i.e., a "V"-shape.

The Rotated Second-Order Cone

A useful variant appears frequently in quadratic programming applications:

Definition 6.1.3 — Rotated Second-Order Cone

The rotated second-order cone is the set:

\[\mathcal{Q}^n_{\mathrm{rot}} := \{(x, y, z) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \times \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} : 2yz \geq \|x\|_2^2\}\]
Intuition: Why "Rotated"?

The rotated cone captures constraints of the form "a product of two non-negative quantities bounds a squared norm." This is exactly the shape you get if you take an ordinary second-order cone and rotate it so that instead of one "height" variable \(c\), you have two non-negative variables \(y\) and \(z\) whose geometric mean controls the norm of \(x\).

Concretely, when \(n = 1\), the constraint \(2yz \geq x^2\) with \(y, z \geq 0\) describes a paraboloid-like surface in 3D space. If you slice at \(y + z = 1\) you get an ellipse.

Crucially, the rotated cone can always be converted to a standard second-order cone via a change of variables:

Theorem 6.1.4 — Rotated ↔ Standard Conversion

A point \((x, y, z)\) belongs to \(\mathcal{Q}^n_{\mathrm{rot}}\) if and only if:

\[\left(\sqrt{2}\,x,\; y - z\right) \in \mathbb{R}^{n+1},\quad y + z \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\]

belongs to the standard cone \(\mathcal{Q}^{n+1}\). The key algebraic step is that \(2yz \geq \|x\|^2\) can be rewritten as \((y+z)^2 \geq (y-z)^2 + 2\|x\|^2\), which is exactly the standard cone condition on the transformed coordinates.

Key Properties: Proper and Self-Dual

The second-order cone has two structural properties that make it especially nice for optimization:

Property: \(\mathcal{Q}^n\) is a Proper Cone

The second-order cone is convex, closed, solid (has non-empty interior), and pointed (contains no line). These four properties together make it a proper cone, which is exactly the kind of cone that supports a well-behaved optimization theory.

Pointedness is easy to see: if both \((x, c)\) and \((-x, -c)\) were in \(\mathcal{Q}^n\), we'd need \(c \geq 0\) and \(-c \geq 0\), forcing \(c = 0\) and then \(\|x\| \leq 0\), so \(x = 0\).

Theorem 6.1.6 — Self-Duality: \((\mathcal{Q}^n)^* = \mathcal{Q}^n\)

The second-order cone is self-dual, meaning the dual cone is the cone itself.

Recall that the dual cone \(K^*\) consists of all vectors that form a non-negative inner product with every element of \(K\). Self-duality is a very special property — it means the cone "looks the same from both sides" of the duality. It is analogous to how the non-negative orthant \(\mathbb{R}^n_{\geq 0}\) is self-dual (which underlies LP duality). Self-duality of \(\mathcal{Q}^n\) will directly give us SOCP duality.

Proof Sketch of Self-Duality

\((\mathcal{Q}^n)^* \subseteq \mathcal{Q}^n\): Take any \((x^*, c^*)\) in the dual cone. Choose specific test points: setting \(x = 0, c = 1\) gives \(c^* \geq 0\); setting \(x = -x^*\) and \(c = \|x^*\|\) gives \(c^* \geq \|x^*\|_2\).

\(\mathcal{Q}^n \subseteq (\mathcal{Q}^n)^*\): For two points \((x, c)\) and \((x^*, c^*)\) both in \(\mathcal{Q}^n\), the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality gives \(\langle x^*, x\rangle \geq -\|x^*\|\|x\|\), so \(c^* c + \langle x^*, x\rangle \geq c^*c - \|x^*\|\|x\| \geq 0\).

6.2 Second-Order Cone Programs

Now we use the second-order cone to define a powerful class of optimization problems. The basic building block is the second-order cone constraint.

Definition 6.2.1 — Second-Order Cone Constraint

A second-order cone constraint (SOC constraint) is an inequality of the form:

\[\|Ax + b\|_2 \leq c^\top x + d\]

for given \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n_0 \times n}\), \(b \in \mathbb{R}^{n_0}\), \(c \in \mathbb{R}^n\), \(d \in \mathbb{R}\).

In words: the Euclidean norm of an affine function of \(x\) is bounded by another affine function of \(x\). This is equivalent to requiring the point \((Ax + b,\; c^\top x + d)\) to lie inside the cone \(\mathcal{Q}^{n_0}\). Notice that if you set \(A = 0\) and \(b = 0\), the constraint becomes \(0 \leq c^\top x + d\), which is just a linear inequality. So SOC constraints generalize linear constraints.

Definition 6.2.2 — Second-Order Cone Program (SOCP)

An SOCP in general form is:

\[\min \; f^\top x \qquad \text{subject to} \quad \|A_i x + b_i\|_2 \leq c_i^\top x + d_i \;\; \text{for } i = 1, \dots, m, \qquad Fx = g.\]

The objective is linear, the constraints are a mix of SOC constraints and linear equalities.

In standard form, we write the same program more compactly by requiring the combined vector \((A_i x + b_i, \; c_i^\top x + d_i)\) to lie in \(\mathcal{Q}^{n_i}\).

Theorem 6.2.3 — Every LP is an SOCP

Every linear program can be written as a second-order cone program. We simply express the linear inequality \(Cx + d \leq 0\) using the SOC constraint \(\|0 \cdot x + 0\|_2 \leq -(C_i x + d_i)\) for each row \(i\), which reduces to a scalar inequality.

The Hierarchy of Conic Programs

SOCPs sit strictly between LPs and SDPs (semidefinite programs) in terms of expressive power. Every LP is an SOCP, every SOCP is an SDP, but not conversely. This hierarchy is one of the central themes of Part III of the course.

SDP (Semidefinite Programs)
⊇
SOCP (Second-Order Cone Programs)
⊇
LP (Linear Programs)

6.3 Duality for SOCPs

Just as LP duality is built on the self-duality of the non-negative orthant, SOCP duality is built on the self-duality of \(\mathcal{Q}^n\). The derivation proceeds via the Lagrangian, introducing dual variables for each constraint and minimizing over the primal variables.

Deriving the Dual (General Form)

We first introduce auxiliary variables \(y_i = A_i x + b_i\) and \(t_i = c_i^\top x + d_i\), converting the SOC constraints to \(\|y_i\| \leq t_i\). The Lagrangian involves multipliers \(\lambda_i\) for the cone constraints, \(\tilde{\nu}_i\) for the equality \(y_i = A_i x + b_i\), \(\bar{\nu}_i\) for \(t_i = c_i^\top x + d_i\), and \(\nu\) for \(Fx = g\). Minimizing term-by-term over the primal variables yields the dual:

Dual of an SOCP (General Form)
\[\max_{\lambda, \tilde{\nu}, \nu} \; -\sum_{i=1}^{m} \left(\tilde{\nu}_i^\top b_i + \lambda_i d_i + \nu_i g_i\right)\]

subject to:

\[\sum_{i=1}^{m} A_i \tilde{\nu}_i^\top + \sum_{i=1}^{m} \bar{\nu}_i c_i - F^\top \nu = f, \qquad \|\tilde{\nu}_i\|_2 \leq \lambda_i.\]

The key observation: the dual of an SOCP is again an SOCP. This mirrors the LP case where the dual of an LP is an LP.

Standard Form Duality

For the standard form, the duality takes an especially clean shape, directly using the self-duality of \(\mathcal{Q}^n\):

Definition 6.3.1 — Standard Form SOCP Duality
PrimalDual
\(\min \; c^\top x\)
\(Ax = b\)
\(x \in \mathcal{Q}^n\)		 \(\max \; b^\top y\)
\(c - A^\top y \in \mathcal{Q}^n\)

Weak duality holds: for any feasible primal \(x\) and dual \(y\), we have \(c^\top x \geq b^\top y\).

Why Weak Duality Works

If \(x \in \mathcal{Q}^n\) and \((c - A^\top y) \in \mathcal{Q}^n\), then by self-duality, \(\langle c - A^\top y, x \rangle \geq 0\). Expanding: \(c^\top x - y^\top Ax \geq 0\). Since \(Ax = b\), this gives \(c^\top x \geq b^\top y\). This is the same trick as in LP duality but using the cone inner product instead of component-wise non-negativity.

6.4 Quadratic Optimization Problems (QP)

One of the most practically important facts about SOCPs is that they capture quadratic programs — problems with a quadratic objective and linear constraints.

Definition 6.4.1 — Quadratic Program (QP)

Let \(Q \in \mathcal{S}^n_+\) be positive semidefinite. A QP in general form is:

\[\min \; x^\top Q x + c^\top x \qquad \text{s.t.} \quad Cx \leq d, \quad Ax = b.\]

Because \(Q\) is PSD, the objective is convex — the "bowl" always curves upward (or is flat in some directions).

How QP Reduces to SOCP

The trick is to introduce an auxiliary variable \(q\) and rewrite:

\[\min \; q + c^\top x \qquad \text{s.t.} \quad x^\top Q x \leq q, \quad Cx \leq d, \quad Ax = b.\]

The constraint \(x^\top Q x \leq q\) is equivalent to \((Q^{1/2} x, \, q, \, \frac{1}{2}) \in \mathcal{Q}^n_{\mathrm{rot}}\). Here's why: since \(Q = Q^{1/2} Q^{1/2}\), we have \(x^\top Q x = \|Q^{1/2} x\|_2^2\), and the rotated cone constraint \(2 \cdot q \cdot \frac{1}{2} \geq \|Q^{1/2} x\|_2^2\) is exactly \(q \geq x^\top Q x\).

Example: Least Squares as an SOCP

The classic least-squares problem \(\min \|Ax - b\|_2^2\) can be rewritten as \(\min\; t\) subject to \(\|Ax - b\|_2 \leq t\), which is directly an SOC constraint with a linear objective. No quadratic objective needed — the norm constraint does the work.

6.5 Quadratically Constrained Quadratic Programs (QCQP)

Going one step further, we can also have quadratic constraints in addition to a quadratic objective:

Definition 6.5.1 — QCQP

With \(Q, Q_1, \dots, Q_{m_1} \in \mathcal{S}^n_+\) all positive semidefinite, the QCQP is:

\[\min \; x^\top Q x + c^\top x \qquad \text{s.t.} \quad x^\top Q_i x + 2p_i^\top x + r_i \leq 0 \; \text{for } i \in [m_1], \quad Cx \leq d, \quad Ax = b.\]

The key insight for reducing QCQP to SOCP is a technique called completing the square. For any PSD matrix \(R\) with vectors \(a\) and scalar \(r\):

\[x^\top R x + 2a^\top x + r = \|R^{1/2} x + R^{-1/2} a\|_2^2 + r - a^\top R^{-1} a.\]

This converts each quadratic constraint into a constraint on a norm, which is exactly an SOC constraint. For example, the \(i\)-th constraint becomes:

\[\|Q_i^{1/2} x + Q_i^{-1/2} p_i\|_2 \leq \sqrt{p_i^\top Q_i^{-1} p_i - r_i}\]

which fits the pattern \(\|Ax + b\| \leq d\) (a constant right-hand side, which is a special case of an SOC constraint).

Important: QCQP ⊊ SOCP

Every QCQP (with PSD constraint matrices) can be written as an SOCP, but not every SOCP can be written as a QCQP. The key issue is that in an SOC constraint \(\|Ax + b\| \leq c^\top x + d\), the right-hand side is an affine function of \(x\). Squaring both sides gives a non-convex constraint (the right side becomes \((c^\top x + d)^2\), which is convex as a function but appears on the "wrong side" of an inequality). So SOCPs are strictly more expressive than QCQPs.

6.6 Applications

Markowitz Portfolio Optimization

One of the most famous applications of quadratic (and hence second-order cone) programming is in finance. Suppose you have a budget \(B\) to invest across \(n\) assets. The variable \(x_i\) represents the fraction of the budget allocated to asset \(i\).

The Deterministic Case

If price changes \(p_i\) are known in advance, you simply maximize \(p^\top x\) subject to \(\frac{1}{B}\mathbf{1}^\top x = 1\) and \(x \geq 0\). The solution is trivial: put everything into the best asset. But this ignores uncertainty.

The Stochastic Case (Markowitz)

When price changes are random with mean vector \(p\) and covariance matrix \(\Sigma\), the expected return is \(p^\top x\) and the risk (variance) is \(x^\top \Sigma x\). The Markowitz model minimizes risk for a target return:

Markowitz Portfolio Optimization (QP)
\[\min \; x^\top \Sigma x \qquad \text{s.t.} \quad p^\top x \geq r, \quad \tfrac{1}{B}\mathbf{1}^\top x = 1, \quad x \geq 0.\]

This is a quadratic program (PSD objective, linear constraints) and therefore an SOCP.

Intuition: The Risk–Return Trade-Off

Imagine two assets: a stable government bond (low return, low variance) and a volatile tech stock (high return, high variance). Putting all money in the stock maximizes expected return but also maximizes risk. Markowitz's insight is that diversification — splitting between assets — can reduce risk below what either asset alone offers, because the assets' price movements may partially cancel out (captured by off-diagonal entries of \(\Sigma\)).

Loss Risk Constraints (SOCP Extension)

If the price change vector \(p\) is Gaussian, we can add a constraint on the probability of an unacceptable return. The constraint \(\Pr(\text{return} < \alpha) \leq \beta\) can be written as:

\[p^\top x + \Phi^{-1}(\beta) \|\Sigma^{1/2} x\|_2 \geq \alpha\]

where \(\Phi^{-1}\) is the inverse CDF of the standard Gaussian. For \(\beta \leq 1/2\) (meaning \(\Phi^{-1}(\beta) \leq 0\)), this is a second-order cone constraint — the norm of a linear function of \(x\) appears on the left-hand side. This extension is beyond what a pure QP can handle and requires the full power of SOCP.

Distance Between Polyhedra

Given two polyhedra \(P_1 = \{x : A_1 x \leq b_1\}\) and \(P_2 = \{x : A_2 x \leq b_2\}\), the minimum Euclidean distance between them is:

\[\mathrm{dist}(P_1, P_2) = \inf\{\|x_1 - x_2\|_2 : x_1 \in P_1,\; x_2 \in P_2\}\]

This can be computed as a quadratic program:

\[\min \; \|x_1 - x_2\|_2^2 \qquad \text{s.t.} \quad A_1 x_1 \leq b_1, \quad A_2 x_2 \leq b_2.\]

The objective \(\|x_1 - x_2\|_2^2\) is a quadratic function of the combined variable \((x_1, x_2)\) with PSD Hessian, so this is a convex QP — and hence solvable as an SOCP.

Summary: The Problem Hierarchy

A central contribution of this chapter is establishing where different optimization problem classes sit relative to each other. The following table summarizes the containment relationships:

Problem Class Objective Constraints Containment
LP Linear Linear LP ⊂ QP
QP Convex quadratic Linear QP ⊂ QCQP
QCQP Convex quadratic Convex quadratic + linear QCQP ⊊ SOCP
SOCP Linear Second-order cone + linear SOCP ⊂ SDP

Note the "trick" in SOCP: the objective is linear, but the SOC constraints are powerful enough to encode quadratic objectives (by introducing auxiliary variables). The expressive power lives in the constraints.

Key Takeaways

  • The second-order (Lorentz) cone \(\mathcal{Q}^n = \{(x,c) : \|x\|_2 \leq c\}\) is a proper, self-dual cone. Self-duality is the foundation for SOCP duality, just as the non-negative orthant's self-duality underlies LP duality.
  • The rotated second-order cone \(\mathcal{Q}^n_{\mathrm{rot}}\) with constraint \(2yz \geq \|x\|^2\) is equivalent to the standard cone via a simple linear change of variables, and is especially convenient for expressing quadratic-over-linear constraints.
  • SOCPs generalize LPs (set \(A = 0, b = 0\) in the SOC constraint) and capture QPs and QCQPs (via the rotated cone and completing the square). The dual of an SOCP is again an SOCP, and weak duality always holds.
  • Practical modeling power: Many real-world problems — portfolio optimization with risk constraints, robust linear programming under uncertainty, distance computations — naturally lead to SOCPs. Efficient solvers (interior-point methods) handle them in polynomial time.
  • SOCPs sit strictly below SDPs in the conic hierarchy: every SOC constraint can be written as a linear matrix inequality (as shown in Chapter 7), but not every SDP is an SOCP.
