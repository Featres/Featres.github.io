Chapter 5: Constrained Optimization

Convex Optimization — Through the Lens of Algorithms and Applications Chapter 5

This chapter addresses the central question: how do we minimize a convex function when there are constraints? In earlier chapters we learned to handle unconstrained problems with gradient descent and Newton's method. Now we extend those tools — first to problems with equality constraints only, and then to the full problem with both equality and inequality constraints. The star of the chapter is the barrier method (also called an interior-point method), which cleverly converts inequality constraints into a smooth penalty term so that we can re-use Newton's method from the inside of the feasible region.

Chapter Roadmap
  1. Section 5.1 — Equality Constrained Minimization: Analytic solutions for quadratics, elimination of constraints, the dual method, and Newton's method adapted for equalities.
  2. Section 5.2 — Inequality Constrained Minimization: The logarithmic barrier function, the central path, the barrier method algorithm, and the Phase I feasibility problem.

The General Constrained Problem

We are interested in solving problems of the form:

Standard Form — Constrained Convex Program (5.1)
$$ \min \; f(x) \qquad \text{s.t.} \quad g_i(x) \le 0 \;\; \forall\, i \in [m], \quad Ax = b $$

where $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ and each $g_i : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ are twice differentiable convex functions, $A \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell \times n}$ with $\operatorname{rank}(A) = \ell < n$, and $b \in \mathbb{R}^\ell$.

Intuition: Think of $f$ as a cost you want to minimize. The inequalities $g_i(x) \le 0$ carve out a feasible region (like walls of a room), and the equalities $Ax = b$ pin you to a particular affine subspace (like being restricted to a specific floor of a building). The goal is to find the cheapest point that lies inside the room and on the correct floor.

When $m = \ell = 0$ (no constraints at all), this reduces to the unconstrained case handled in Chapter 4. The chapter tackles increasing generality: first $m = 0$ (equality constraints only), then the full problem.

5.1 — Equality Constrained Minimization

We first consider the simpler sub-problem with equality constraints only:

$$ \min \; f(x) \qquad \text{s.t.} \quad Ax = b $$

The assumption $\operatorname{rank}(A) = \ell < n$ means there are fewer independent constraints than variables, so the feasible set is a non-trivial affine subspace. Four approaches are discussed.

Method 1: The Analytic Method

When $f$ is a convex quadratic, the problem can be solved exactly by writing down the KKT optimality conditions and solving a system of linear equations.

Example 5.1.1 — Quadratic Objective

For $f(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}x^\top P x + q^\top x + r$ with $P \succeq 0$, the KKT conditions give the linear system:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} P & A^\top \\ A & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x^* \\ v^* \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -q \\ b \end{bmatrix} $$

Intuition: This is a block matrix system of $n + \ell$ equations in $n + \ell$ unknowns. The top block enforces the optimality condition (gradient of the Lagrangian is zero) and the bottom block enforces feasibility ($Ax = b$). If $P$ is positive definite, there is a unique solution. If $P$ is only positive semidefinite (has zero eigenvalues), any solution to the system is optimal. If no solution exists, the problem is unbounded below.

Method 2: Eliminating Equality Constraints

The idea is to "substitute away" the equality constraints and reduce to an unconstrained problem. We find a matrix $F \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times (n-\ell)}$ whose columns span the null space of $A$, and any particular solution $\hat{x}$ satisfying $A\hat{x} = b$. Then every feasible point can be written as $x = Fz + \hat{x}$ for some $z \in \mathbb{R}^{n-\ell}$.

$$ \{x \mid Ax = b\} = \{Fz + \hat{x} \mid z \in \mathbb{R}^{n-\ell}\} $$

We then solve the unconstrained problem $\min_z f(Fz + \hat{x})$ using Newton's method and recover $x^* = Fz^* + \hat{x}$.

Intuition

Imagine you must stay on a flat surface (the equality constraint). Instead of optimizing in 3D with a constraint, you set up a 2D coordinate system on that surface and optimize freely in those 2 coordinates. That's exactly what the null-space parameterization does — it reduces the dimension and removes the constraint entirely.

Method 3: The Dual Method

If strong duality holds (e.g., Slater's condition is satisfied), we can solve the dual problem instead. The dual function for the equality constrained problem is:

$$ L_b(\nu) = -b^\top \nu - f^*(-A^\top \nu) $$

where $f^*$ is the conjugate (Legendre transform) of $f$. If $L_b$ is twice differentiable, we can maximize it using unconstrained methods to get an optimal dual variable $\nu^*$. Then we recover the primal optimum by minimizing the Lagrangian $L(x, \nu^*)$ over $x$. If that minimizer is primal feasible, it's also primal optimal by strong duality.

Method 4: Feasible Start Newton's Method

This is often the preferred method in practice. The idea is to adapt Newton's method to stay on the feasible set $\{x \mid Ax = b\}$ at every iteration.

Recall that in unconstrained Newton's method, the Newton step is obtained by minimizing a second-order Taylor approximation. In the equality-constrained case, we minimize the same Taylor approximation subject to the constraint that the step keeps us feasible:

$$ \min_v \; \tilde{f}(x^{(k)} + v) = f(x^{(k)}) + \nabla f(x^{(k)})^\top v + \tfrac{1}{2} v^\top \nabla^2 f(x^{(k)}) v \qquad \text{s.t.} \;\; A(x^{(k)} + v) = b $$

Since $x^{(k)}$ is already feasible ($Ax^{(k)} = b$), the constraint simplifies to $Av = 0$. The Newton step $\Delta x_N$ is characterized by the KKT system:

Newton Step for Equality-Constrained Problems
$$ \begin{bmatrix} \nabla^2 f(x) & A^\top \\ A & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \Delta x_N \\ w \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -\nabla f(x) \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

where $w$ is the dual variable associated with the equality constraint.

Intuition: At each step we ask: "Given where I am, what's the best direction to move while staying on the feasible plane $Ax = b$?" The top row gives the optimality condition and the bottom row ($A \Delta x_N = 0$) ensures the step doesn't leave the feasible set.

The Newton decrement $\lambda(x)^2 = -\nabla f(x)^\top \Delta x_N$ serves as the stopping criterion, just as in the unconstrained case. If $f$ is quadratic, the method solves the problem in a single iteration.

Theorem 5.1.2 — Convergence of Feasible Start Newton's Method

If $f$ is strongly convex and self-concordant, the feasible start Newton's method with backtracking line search achieves $f(x^{(k)}) - f^* \le \varepsilon$ in at most

$$ k \ge \frac{f(x^{(0)}) - f^*}{\gamma} + \log \log \frac{1}{\varepsilon} $$

steps, where $\gamma$ is a constant from the self-concordance theory (Theorem 4.7.10). This is the same convergence rate as unconstrained Newton's method.

Variant: The Infeasible Start Newton's Method

What if we don't have a feasible starting point? The infeasible start variant modifies the Newton step so that it simultaneously tries to reduce the objective and reduce infeasibility. The KKT system becomes:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} \nabla^2 f(x) & A^\top \\ A & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \Delta x_N \\ w \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -\nabla f(x) \\ b - Ax \end{bmatrix} $$

Notice the right-hand side: the bottom block is now $b - Ax$ (the "residual" of infeasibility) instead of $0$. When $x$ is feasible, this reduces exactly to the feasible-start version.

5.2 — Inequality Constrained Minimization

Now we tackle the full problem (5.1) with both equality and inequality constraints. We assume the problem is strictly feasible (Slater's condition holds), meaning there exists a point $x$ with $Ax = b$ and $g_i(x) < 0$ for all $i$. This guarantees zero duality gap and that KKT conditions are necessary and sufficient.

The Core Idea: From Constraints to Penalties

We already know how to solve equality-constrained problems. So the strategy is: convert inequality constraints into a penalty term in the objective, reducing the problem to the equality-constrained case we already know how to handle.

The natural first attempt uses indicator functions. Define $I_-(u) = 0$ if $u \le 0$ and $I_-(u) = +\infty$ if $u > 0$. Then problem (5.1) is equivalent to:

$$ \min \; f(x) + \sum_{i=1}^{m} I_-(g_i(x)) \qquad \text{s.t.} \;\; Ax = b $$

This is now an equality-constrained problem — great! But the indicator function is not differentiable (it jumps from $0$ to $\infty$), so we can't use Newton's method.

The fix: replace the sharp indicator $I_-$ with a smooth approximation — the logarithmic barrier function.

The Logarithmic Barrier Function

Definition 5.2.1 — Logarithmic Barrier (single constraint)

For $t > 0$, define:

$$ \tilde{I}_-(x) := -\frac{1}{t} \log(-x), \qquad x < 0 $$

This is a smooth, convex approximation to the indicator function $I_-(x)$.

Intuition: The $-\log(-x)$ function is zero-ish when $x$ is very negative (deep inside the feasible region) and blows up to $+\infty$ as $x \to 0^-$ (approaching the boundary). The parameter $t$ controls how sharply it approximates the true indicator — larger $t$ means a tighter approximation that looks more like the step function.

Proposition 5.2.2 — Properties

For every $t > 0$, the logarithmic barrier $\tilde{I}_-(x)$ is convex and twice continuously differentiable on $(-\infty, 0)$, with first derivative $-1/(tx)$ and second derivative $1/(tx^2)$.

For the full problem with $m$ inequality constraints, the barrier function aggregates all constraints:

Definition 5.2.3 — Logarithmic Barrier Function (multiple constraints)
$$ \phi(x) := -\sum_{i=1}^{m} \log(-g_i(x)) $$

Domain: the set of strictly feasible points $\{x \mid g_i(x) < 0 \;\forall i\}$.

The approximated problem, parameterized by $t > 0$, becomes:

$$ \min \; f(x) + \frac{1}{t}\, \phi(x) \qquad \text{s.t.} \;\; Ax = b \tag{5.5} $$

This is a convex program with only equality constraints — solvable by the methods of Section 5.1!

Key Observations
  • Larger $t$ → better approximation to the original problem (the barrier becomes a steeper wall).
  • Larger $t$ → the Hessian of $\phi$ varies more wildly near the constraint boundaries, which can slow down Newton's method.
  • The gradient and Hessian of $\phi$ are: $\nabla \phi(x) = \sum_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{-g_i(x)} \nabla g_i(x)$ and $\nabla^2 \phi(x) = \sum_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{g_i(x)^2} \nabla g_i(x) \nabla g_i(x)^\top + \sum_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{-g_i(x)} \nabla^2 g_i(x)$.

The Central Path

As we vary $t$ from small to large, the minimizer of the approximate problem (5.5) traces a curve through the interior of the feasible region. This curve is called the central path.

Definition 5.2.4 — Central Path

For the equivalent formulation $\min\; t\,f(x) + \phi(x)$ subject to $Ax = b$, the set of minimizers $\{x^*_t \mid t > 0\}$ is called the central path.

(Multiplying $f$ by $t$ does not change the minimizers, only the objective value.)

Example 5.2.5 — Central Path in 1D

Consider $\min\, -x$ subject to $x \le 0$. The optimal solution is $x^* = 0$ with value $0$.

The approximate problem is $\min\; -x - \tfrac{1}{t}\log(-x)$. Setting the derivative to zero:

$$ -1 - \frac{1}{tx} \cdot (-1) = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x^*_t = -\frac{1}{t} $$

As $t \to \infty$, the central path $x^*_t = -1/t \to 0$, converging to the true optimum. Each point on the central path sits strictly inside the feasible region (here $x < 0$), getting closer and closer to the boundary.

Important properties of points on the central path:

Proposition 5.2.6 — Necessary Conditions for Central Path

Every point $x^*_t$ on the central path satisfies: $Ax^*_t = b$ and $g_i(x^*_t) < 0$ for all $i$.

Why? The equality constraints hold because $x^*_t$ is a minimizer of a problem with those constraints. The inequality constraints are strictly satisfied because hitting the boundary ($g_i = 0$) would send $\log(-g_i) \to -\infty$, making $\phi \to +\infty$ — impossible for a minimizer.

Connection to KKT Conditions

A point $x_t$ lies on the central path if and only if there exist dual variables $\lambda, \nu$ satisfying conditions that look almost like the KKT conditions:

Modified KKT Conditions for the Central Path (5.7)
$$ \begin{aligned} g_i(x) &\le 0 \quad \forall\, i &\text{(primal feasibility)} \\ Ax - b &= 0 &\text{(equality feasibility)} \\ \lambda_i &\ge 0 \quad \forall\, i &\text{(dual feasibility)} \\ -\lambda_i \, g_i(x) &= \tfrac{1}{t} \quad \forall\, i &\text{(approximate complementarity)} \\ \nabla f(x) + \textstyle\sum_i \lambda_i \nabla g_i(x) + A^\top \nu &= 0 &\text{(stationarity)} \end{aligned} $$

Intuition: These are exactly the standard KKT conditions except for the fourth line. In true KKT, complementary slackness requires $\lambda_i g_i(x) = 0$ (either the constraint is inactive or the multiplier is zero). Here, the product equals $-1/t$ instead of $0$. As $t \to \infty$, we recover exact KKT conditions. So the central path is a smooth family of "approximately KKT" points converging to the true optimum.

Suboptimality Bound via Duality

From each central path point $x^*_t$, we can construct a dual feasible point:

Proposition 5.2.9 — Dual Certificate

For each $x^*_t$ on the central path, define:

$$ (\lambda^*_t)_i = \frac{-1}{t \cdot g_i(x^*_t)}, \qquad \nu^*_t = \frac{\nu}{t} $$

Then $(\lambda^*_t, \nu^*_t)$ is a feasible dual point, and the duality gap satisfies:

$$ f(x^*_t) - f^* \;\le\; \frac{m}{t} $$

where $m$ is the number of inequality constraints.

Intuition: This is a remarkably clean bound. It says the suboptimality is at most $m/t$. Want an $\varepsilon$-optimal solution? Just make $t \ge m/\varepsilon$. The more constraints you have, the larger $t$ needs to be. This motivates the algorithm below.

The Barrier Method

A naive approach would be to simply set $t = m/\varepsilon$ and solve one approximate problem. This works in theory but can be very slow: for large $t$, the Hessian is ill-conditioned and Newton's method converges poorly.

The barrier method is smarter: it solves a sequence of approximate problems for progressively increasing values of $t$, using each solution as the starting point for the next. This "warm start" strategy makes each subproblem much easier.

Algorithm 7 — The Barrier Method

Input: Strictly feasible point $x^*$, initial $t = t^{(0)} > 0$, growth factor $\mu > 1$, tolerance $\varepsilon > 0$.

Output: A point $x$ with $f(x) - f^* \le \varepsilon$.

  1. Centering step: Starting from current $x^*$, solve $\min\; t\,f(x) + \phi(x)$ s.t. $Ax = b$ (using Newton's method) to get the central path point $x^*_t$.
  2. Update: Set $x^* \leftarrow x^*_t$.
  3. Check: If $m/t \le \varepsilon$, stop and return $x^*$.
  4. Increase $t$: Set $t \leftarrow \mu \cdot t$.
  5. Go to Step 1.

Terminology: Each execution of the centering step (solving one approximate problem via Newton) is called an inner iteration. Each loop through Steps 1–4 is called an outer iteration.

Trade-offs in Parameter Choices
  • Growth factor $\mu$: Large $\mu$ → fewer outer iterations, but each centering step (inner iteration) requires more Newton steps because the starting point is farther from the new optimum. Small $\mu$ → each centering step is cheap, but you need many outer iterations.
  • Initial $t^{(0)}$: Large $t^{(0)}$ → the first centering step is expensive. Small $t^{(0)}$ → more outer iterations needed.
Proposition 5.2.10 — Convergence of the Barrier Method

For every $\varepsilon > 0$, the barrier method achieves $f(x^*) - f^* \le \varepsilon$ after at most $k$ outer iterations, where:

$$ k \ge \frac{\log\!\bigl(\frac{m}{\varepsilon\, t^{(0)}}\bigr)}{\log \mu} + 1 $$

Intuition: The number of outer iterations grows logarithmically in the desired accuracy $1/\varepsilon$ and the number of constraints $m$. This is very efficient! Combined with the fast (quadratic/superlinear) convergence of Newton's method for each inner iteration, the barrier method is a powerful interior-point algorithm.

Worked Example — Putting it Together

Suppose you have $m = 100$ constraints, initial $t^{(0)} = 1$, growth factor $\mu = 10$, and want $\varepsilon = 0.01$ accuracy.

Number of outer iterations: $k \ge \frac{\log(100 / 0.01)}{\log 10} + 1 = \frac{\log(10{,}000)}{\log 10} + 1 = 4 + 1 = 5$.

So only about 5 outer iterations (each involving a Newton solve) suffice. This is the power of the barrier method.

Phase I: Finding a Feasible Starting Point

The barrier method requires a strictly feasible starting point — a point where all inequality constraints hold strictly. Finding such a point is itself an optimization problem, called Phase I. (Running the barrier method itself is called Phase II.)

Basic Phase I Method

Introduce a slack variable $s$ and solve:

$$ \min \; s \qquad \text{s.t.} \;\; g_i(x) \le s \;\;\forall\, i, \quad Ax = b $$

This is easy to initialize: pick any $x$ in the domain and set $s > \max_i g_i(x)$. Then apply the barrier method to this auxiliary problem.

Interpreting the Result
  • $s^* < 0$: The original problem is strictly feasible — any $x$ with $s < 0$ gives a valid starting point. (No need to solve to optimality; stop as soon as $s$ becomes negative.)
  • $s^* > 0$: The original problem is infeasible — no point satisfies all constraints.
  • $s^* = 0$: The problem is feasible but not strictly feasible — a delicate edge case that can be difficult to handle numerically.

Phase I via Infeasible Start Newton's Method

An alternative is to rewrite the original problem with an extra variable $s$ and enforce $s = 0$ as an equality constraint. The infeasible start Newton's method can then be initialized with any $x$ in the domain and $s > \max_i g_i(x)$, and it will simultaneously drive toward feasibility ($s \to 0$) and optimality.

Key Takeaways

  • Reduction strategy: The whole chapter is about reducing harder problems to easier ones — inequality constraints are converted to equality-constrained problems via the log barrier, and equality-constrained problems can be reduced to unconstrained ones via null-space elimination or handled directly with adapted Newton's method.
  • The logarithmic barrier function $\phi(x) = -\sum_i \log(-g_i(x))$ is a smooth, convex approximation to the hard indicator function, parameterized by $t$. Larger $t$ means a better approximation.
  • The central path is the trajectory of minimizers $x^*_t$ as $t$ increases. It satisfies "approximate KKT" conditions with complementarity gap $1/t$, yielding the suboptimality bound $f(x^*_t) - f^* \le m/t$.
  • The barrier method follows the central path by solving a sequence of centering problems with increasing $t$. It needs only $O\!\bigl(\frac{\log(m/\varepsilon)}{\log \mu}\bigr)$ outer iterations — logarithmic in both $m$ and $1/\varepsilon$.
  • Phase I methods handle the practical issue of finding a strictly feasible starting point, either by introducing a slack variable or using the infeasible-start Newton variant.
