Lecture 4 — Properties of the MLE, Method of Moments & the EM Algorithm

Course: Core Concepts in Statistical Learning Lecture 4 Date: 2026-04-13

Overview. This lecture deepens our understanding of the Maximum Likelihood Estimator (MLE). We first see an alternative (and often more convenient) formula for the Fisher Information, then extend the asymptotic normality result to the multivariate case. We then answer a natural question: why is maximizing the likelihood a good idea? The answer involves the Kullback–Leibler divergence and Jensen's inequality. Finally, we introduce two more estimation techniques: the Method of Moments, a simple alternative to MLE, and the EM Algorithm, a powerful trick for maximizing likelihoods that are otherwise hard to handle (e.g. in mixture models).

1. An Alternative Formula for the Fisher Information

Recall from Lecture 3 that the Fisher Information $I(\theta_0)$ governs how precisely we can estimate a parameter: the larger $I(\theta_0)$, the smaller the asymptotic variance of the MLE. It was defined as the variance of the score function:

$$I(\theta_0) = \mathbb{V}_{\theta_0}(s(X; \theta_0)) = \mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[s(X; \theta_0)^2\right].$$

If the log-density $\theta \mapsto \log f(x, \theta)$ is twice continuously differentiable, we can rewrite it using the second derivative:

Alternative Formula for Fisher Information
$$I(\theta_0) = -\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[\left.\frac{\partial^2 \log f(X, \theta)}{\partial \theta^2}\right|_{\theta = \theta_0}\right].$$

In words: the Fisher Information equals the negative expected curvature of the log-likelihood at the true parameter.

Intuition — curvature = information

Think about what the log-likelihood looks like near its maximum $\theta_0$. If it is sharply peaked (large negative second derivative, i.e. high curvature), then the data strongly pinpoint $\theta_0$ — small changes in $\theta$ cause big drops in likelihood. If it is flat, many values of $\theta$ are nearly as plausible and we have little information. The Fisher Information measures exactly this "sharpness".

Example — Bernoulli revisited

For $f(x;\theta) = \theta^x(1-\theta)^{1-x}$, we already computed

$$\frac{\partial \log f(x,\theta)}{\partial \theta} = \frac{x}{\theta} - \frac{1-x}{1-\theta}.$$

Differentiating again:

$$\frac{\partial^2 \log f(x,\theta)}{\partial \theta^2} = -\frac{x}{\theta^2} - \frac{1-x}{(1-\theta)^2}.$$

Taking expectations with $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}(X) = \theta_0$:

$$-\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[\frac{\partial^2 \log f}{\partial \theta^2}\right]_{\theta_0} = \frac{\theta_0}{\theta_0^2} + \frac{1-\theta_0}{(1-\theta_0)^2} = \frac{1}{\theta_0} + \frac{1}{1-\theta_0} = \frac{1}{\theta_0(1-\theta_0)}.$$

Same answer as before — but notice how much cleaner the computation was. This is the practical reason we care about the second-derivative form.

2. Asymptotic Normality of the MLE in the Multivariate Case

So far we treated $\theta$ as a single real number. In practice, models often have several parameters (e.g. both a mean and a variance). Assume now that $\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^k$ for some $k \geq 2$.

The score function is now a vector — the gradient of the log-density:

$$s(x, \theta_0) = \nabla_\theta \log f(x, \theta)\big|_{\theta = \theta_0} = \left(\frac{\partial \log f}{\partial \theta_1}, \dots, \frac{\partial \log f}{\partial \theta_k}\right)^{\!\top}_{\!\theta = \theta_0}.$$

Under regularity assumptions, $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}[s(X,\theta_0)] = 0$ (the score has mean zero at the truth), and:

Multivariate Asymptotic Normality of the MLE
$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, I(\theta_0)^{-1}\right)$$

where the Fisher Information Matrix is the $k \times k$ matrix

$$I(\theta_0) = \mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[s(X,\theta_0)\, s(X,\theta_0)^\top\right] = \mathbb{V}_{\theta_0}\!\left(s(X,\theta_0)\right).$$

If the log-density is twice continuously differentiable:

$$I(\theta_0) = -\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial^2 \log f(X,\theta)}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j}\right)_{1 \leq i,j \leq k}\right]_{\theta_0}.$$
Why the inverse?

In one dimension, a larger $I(\theta_0)$ meant a smaller asymptotic variance $1/I(\theta_0)$. In several dimensions, "dividing" becomes "inverting a matrix". High information (sharp log-likelihood in every direction) means a small covariance matrix. The off-diagonal entries of $I(\theta_0)^{-1}$ tell us how estimates of different parameters are correlated.

Example — Gaussian with unknown mean and variance

Let $X_1, \dots, X_n \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$ with $\theta_0 = (\mu_0, \sigma_0^2) \in \mathbb{R} \times (0, \infty)$.

The MLE can be shown to be

$$\hat{\theta}_n = \left(\bar{X}_n,\; \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2\right),$$

i.e. the sample mean and sample variance. The Fisher Information Matrix is diagonal:

$$I(\theta_0) = \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{1}{\sigma_0^2} & 0 \\[6pt] 0 & \dfrac{2}{\sigma_0^2} \end{pmatrix}, \qquad I(\theta_0)^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_0^2 & 0 \\[6pt] 0 & \dfrac{\sigma_0^2}{2} \end{pmatrix}.$$

Therefore

$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(0, \begin{pmatrix}\sigma_0^2 & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_0^2/2\end{pmatrix}\right).$$

The diagonal form is meaningful: asymptotically, the errors in estimating $\mu_0$ and $\sigma_0^2$ are uncorrelated. The first entry $\sigma_0^2$ is exactly the CLT's answer for the sample mean.

3. Why is the MLE a Good Estimator?

We have claimed several times that the MLE is "good". But why should maximizing a product of densities produce a sensible estimate of the truth? The argument uses a beautiful application of Jensen's inequality and links the MLE to the Kullback–Leibler divergence.

A sharper Jensen

Jensen's Inequality under strict convexity

If $g$ is a strictly convex function and $X$ is a random variable, then

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \geq g(\mathbb{E}[X]),$$

with equality if and only if $\mathbb{P}(X = \mathbb{E}[X]) = 1$ (i.e. $X$ is almost surely constant).

The "equality-iff-constant" part is what makes the argument below work. It turns an inequality into a useful characterization.

Identifiability

Identifiability

A parameter $\theta_0$ is identifiable if different parameters give rise to different distributions:

if $\theta \in \Theta$ satisfies $\mathbb{P}_{\theta_0}\!\left(f(X,\theta) = f(X,\theta_0)\right) = 1$, then $\theta = \theta_0$.

Intuition — you can't estimate what you can't distinguish

If two different parameter values $\theta \neq \theta_0$ produce the exact same distribution of the data, no amount of data can ever tell them apart. Identifiability is the minimal assumption that makes estimation meaningful.

The main argument

Maximizing $\ell_n(\theta)$ is the same as maximizing $\tfrac{1}{n}(\ell_n(\theta) - \ell_n(\theta_0))$ (we just subtracted a constant in $\theta$). For any fixed $\theta$:

$$\frac{1}{n}\bigl(\ell_n(\theta) - \ell_n(\theta_0)\bigr) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \log\!\left(\frac{f(X_i,\theta)}{f(X_i,\theta_0)}\right) \xrightarrow{\ \mathbb{P}\ } \mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[\log\!\left(\frac{f(X,\theta)}{f(X,\theta_0)}\right)\right].$$

by the Weak Law of Large Numbers. The right-hand side is (minus) the Kullback–Leibler divergence from $f(\cdot,\theta_0)$ to $f(\cdot,\theta)$.

Now apply Jensen's inequality to the concave function $\log$ (equivalently, convex $-\log$):

$$\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[\log\frac{f(X,\theta)}{f(X,\theta_0)}\right] \leq \log \mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[\frac{f(X,\theta)}{f(X,\theta_0)}\right].$$

But the inner expectation equals 1:

$$\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[\frac{f(X,\theta)}{f(X,\theta_0)}\right] = \int \frac{f(x,\theta)}{f(x,\theta_0)} f(x,\theta_0)\, d\mu(x) = \int f(x,\theta)\, d\mu(x) = 1.$$

So we have, for every $\theta \in \Theta$:

$$\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[\log\frac{f(X,\theta)}{f(X,\theta_0)}\right] \leq \log(1) = 0.$$

The strict-convexity clause says equality holds if and only if the ratio $f(X,\theta)/f(X,\theta_0)$ is almost surely constant (and hence equal to 1, since its expectation is 1). By identifiability, this happens only at $\theta = \theta_0$.

The conclusion

The limit in probability of $\frac{1}{n}(\ell_n(\theta) - \ell_n(\theta_0))$ is

  • $\leq 0$ for all $\theta$, and
  • $= 0$ uniquely at $\theta = \theta_0$.

So asymptotically, the thing we are maximizing is uniquely maximized at the truth. This justifies the strategy "pick $\theta$ that maximizes $\ell_n$".

Intuition — KL divergence as a "distance"

$\mathrm{KL}(f_{\theta_0} \,\|\, f_\theta) := -\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}\!\left[\log \tfrac{f(X,\theta)}{f(X,\theta_0)}\right]$ is a non-negative quantity measuring how different two distributions are, equal to zero only when they coincide. Maximizing the likelihood is, in the limit, equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence from the true distribution to the model. The MLE is picking the model distribution closest to reality in the KL sense.

4. Method of Moments

MLE is powerful but can be computationally nasty (no closed form, tricky optimization). The Method of Moments is a simpler alternative that often provides a quick first estimator — and sometimes even matches the MLE.

The idea

If a distribution has $k$ unknown parameters $\theta = (\theta_1, \dots, \theta_k)$, it is usually characterized by its first $k$ moments. The strategy is:

  1. Compute the theoretical moments $m_j(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\theta[X^j]$ for $j = 1, \dots, k$ as functions of $\theta$.
  2. Estimate the moments empirically: $\hat{m}_j = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^j$.
  3. Solve the system $m_j(\theta) = \hat{m}_j$ for $j = 1, \dots, k$ to get the estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$.
Method of Moments Estimator

The moment estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ is any solution of the system

$$\begin{cases} m_1(\theta) = \bar{X}_n \\ \quad \vdots \\ m_k(\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^k \end{cases}$$

i.e. we equate theoretical moments to empirical moments, and solve for $\theta$.

Intuition — match what you see to what the model predicts

If your model is correct with parameter $\theta_0$, then by the LLN the sample mean should be close to $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}(X)$, the sample second moment close to $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_0}(X^2)$, and so on. So just pretend they are equal and solve for $\theta$. It's a "plug and chug" approach: no optimization, just algebra.

Examples

Example 1 — Bernoulli

For $X \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(\theta)$, the first moment is $m_1(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\theta(X) = \theta$. Setting $m_1(\hat{\theta}_n) = \bar{X}_n$ gives

$$\hat{\theta}_n = \bar{X}_n.$$

This matches the MLE exactly! For Bernoulli, the two methods agree.

Example 2 — Gaussian with unknown mean and variance

For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, set $\theta = (\mu, \sigma)$. We have

$$m_1(\theta) = \mu, \qquad m_2(\theta) = \mathbb{V}_\theta(X) + \mathbb{E}_\theta(X)^2 = \sigma^2 + \mu^2.$$

The system is

$$\begin{cases}(1)\quad \mu = \bar{X}_n \\ (2)\quad \sigma^2 + \mu^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2\end{cases}$$

Plug (1) into (2) and solve:

$$\hat{\sigma}_n^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 - \bar{X}_n^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2.$$

So $\hat{\theta}_n = \left(\bar{X}_n,\sqrt{\tfrac{1}{n}\sum (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2}\right)$ — again, the same as the MLE.

Consistency and asymptotic normality

Suppose the map $\mathbf{m}(\theta) = (m_1(\theta), \dots, m_k(\theta))^\top$ has a continuously differentiable inverse $g = \mathbf{m}^{-1}$. Then the method of moments estimator can be written as

$$\hat{\theta}_n = g\!\left(\bar{X}_n,\, \tfrac{1}{n}\sum X_i^2,\, \dots,\, \tfrac{1}{n}\sum X_i^k\right).$$

By the LLN and the Continuous Mapping Theorem, this converges in probability to $g(\mathbf{m}(\theta_0)) = \theta_0$ — so the estimator is consistent.

By the multivariate CLT, the empirical moments satisfy

$$\sqrt{n}\!\left(\begin{pmatrix}\bar{X}_n \\ \vdots \\ \tfrac{1}{n}\sum X_i^k\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}m_1(\theta_0) \\ \vdots \\ m_k(\theta_0)\end{pmatrix}\right) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma),$$

where $\Sigma = \mathbb{V}\!\left((X, X^2, \dots, X^k)^\top\right)$. Applying the delta method with the smooth function $g$:

Asymptotic Normality of the Method of Moments
$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(0, J_{\theta_0} \Sigma J_{\theta_0}^\top\right)$$

where $J_{\theta_0}$ is the Jacobian of $g$ evaluated at $\mathbf{m}(\theta_0)$.

Trade-off with MLE

Method of Moments is typically easier to compute and always gives a closed form. But the MLE is generally more efficient, meaning it has smaller asymptotic variance (this is the Cramér–Rao bound coming into play). For simple models like Bernoulli and Gaussian the two coincide; for more complex models, MLE is usually preferred — provided you can compute it.

5. Special Topic: The EM Algorithm

Sometimes the log-likelihood is just too painful to maximize directly. A classical case is mixture models, where each observation is drawn from one of several underlying sub-populations, but we don't know which one.

Motivating example: a 2-component mixture

Consider the density

$$f(x, \theta) = p\,\varphi(x - \mu_1) + (1-p)\,\varphi(x - \mu_2), \quad x \in \mathbb{R},$$

where $\theta = (p, \mu_1, \mu_2) \in (0,1) \times \mathbb{R}^2$ and $\varphi$ is some known density (think of it as a standard Normal density). Each observation $X_i$ is drawn either from a distribution centered at $\mu_1$ (with probability $p$) or at $\mu_2$ (with probability $1-p$).

The log-likelihood is

$$\ell_n(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log\!\bigl(p\,\varphi(X_i - \mu_1) + (1-p)\,\varphi(X_i - \mu_2)\bigr).$$
Why this is hard

The log of a sum doesn't split nicely, so you can't separate the optimization over $\mu_1$, $\mu_2$, and $p$. There's no closed-form maximum. The EM algorithm is the standard workaround.

The key idea: latent variables

Imagine that for each $X_i$, there is a hidden label $Z_i \in \{1, 2\}$ telling us which component it came from. If we could observe the $Z_i$, optimization would be easy: we'd just compute the mean of the $X_i$ with $Z_i = 1$ to estimate $\mu_1$, and so on.

The EM algorithm formalizes this "if only we knew the $Z_i$" idea by iterating between:

  • E-step: compute the expected value of the hidden labels given current parameters.
  • M-step: maximize a new, easier objective built from those expectations.

The complete likelihood

Suppose $(X, Z)$ has joint density $f((x,z), \theta)$. The conditional density of $Z$ given $X = x$ is

$$f(z \mid x, \theta) = \frac{f((x,z), \theta)}{f(x, \theta)}.$$

If we had observed the $Z_i$ too, the complete likelihood would be

$$L_n(\theta \mid (X_1, Z_1), \dots, (X_n, Z_n)) = \prod_{i=1}^n f((X_i, Z_i), \theta).$$

Dividing the complete likelihood by the real likelihood and taking logs gives the identity

$$\log L_n(\theta \mid X_1, \dots, X_n) = \log L_n(\theta \mid \text{complete data}) - \log\!\prod_{i=1}^n f(Z_i \mid X_i, \theta).$$

Fix some parameter $\theta^*$ and take the expectation of both sides with respect to the conditional distribution of the $Z_i$ given $X_i$ under $\theta^*$. The left side is a constant in $Z$, so it stays the same. The right side becomes two functions:

The EM decomposition
$$\log L_n(\theta \mid X_1, \dots, X_n) = Q(\theta \mid \theta^*) - H(\theta \mid \theta^*)$$

where

  • $Q(\theta \mid \theta^*)$ is the expected complete-data log-likelihood, and
  • $H(\theta \mid \theta^*)$ comes from the conditional entropy of the latent variables.

The function $Q$ is the easy one; $H$ is ignored during optimization.

The algorithm

EM Algorithm
  1. Initialize with some guess $\hat\theta^{(0)}$.
  2. E-step (at iteration $j$): compute the function $\theta \mapsto Q(\theta \mid \hat\theta^{(j)})$ — the expected complete-data log-likelihood given the current parameters.
  3. M-step: set $\hat\theta^{(j+1)} = \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} Q(\theta \mid \hat\theta^{(j)})$.
  4. Stop when $\|\hat\theta^{(j+1)} - \hat\theta^{(j)}\| < \varepsilon$ for some tolerance $\varepsilon > 0$.

Why it works — monotone improvement

The remarkable property of EM is that every iteration never decreases the likelihood:

$$L_n(\hat\theta^{(j+1)} \mid X_1, \dots, X_n) \geq L_n(\hat\theta^{(j)} \mid X_1, \dots, X_n).$$

Using the decomposition with $\theta^* = \hat\theta^{(j)}$:

$$\log L_n(\hat\theta^{(j+1)}) - \log L_n(\hat\theta^{(j)}) = \underbrace{[Q(\hat\theta^{(j+1)} \mid \hat\theta^{(j)}) - Q(\hat\theta^{(j)} \mid \hat\theta^{(j)})]}_{\geq 0 \text{ by the M-step}} - \underbrace{[H(\hat\theta^{(j+1)} \mid \hat\theta^{(j)}) - H(\hat\theta^{(j)} \mid \hat\theta^{(j)})]}_{\leq 0 \text{ by Jensen}}.$$

The first bracket is non-negative because the M-step explicitly maximizes $Q(\cdot \mid \hat\theta^{(j)})$. The second bracket is non-positive by Jensen's inequality applied to $\log$ (again!). Both signs combine to give $\geq 0$ — the likelihood is monotonically non-decreasing.

Intuition — climbing the likelihood

Think of $Q(\theta \mid \hat\theta^{(j)})$ as a surrogate objective that is easier than the real log-likelihood and lower-bounds it (up to a constant). The M-step maximizes this surrogate. Because the surrogate touches the true log-likelihood at $\hat\theta^{(j)}$, moving up on the surrogate guarantees at least as much progress on the real log-likelihood. Repeating this climbs steadily up the likelihood surface.

Caveat — local maxima only

EM only guarantees non-decrease, not convergence to the global maximum. Different starting points $\hat\theta^{(0)}$ may lead to different local maxima, especially for mixture models. It's standard practice to run EM several times from random initializations and keep the best result.

Example — Gaussian mixture

If $\varphi$ is the density of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, the E-step reduces to computing, for each $i$, the "responsibility"

$$\gamma_i^{(j)} = \mathbb{P}\!\left(Z_i = 1 \mid X_i,\, \hat\theta^{(j)}\right) = \frac{\hat{p}^{(j)}\varphi(X_i - \hat\mu_1^{(j)})}{\hat{p}^{(j)}\varphi(X_i - \hat\mu_1^{(j)}) + (1-\hat{p}^{(j)})\varphi(X_i - \hat\mu_2^{(j)})},$$

i.e. the current best guess of the probability that $X_i$ came from component 1. The M-step then updates

$$\hat{p}^{(j+1)} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \gamma_i^{(j)}, \qquad \hat\mu_1^{(j+1)} = \frac{\sum_i \gamma_i^{(j)} X_i}{\sum_i \gamma_i^{(j)}}, \qquad \hat\mu_2^{(j+1)} = \frac{\sum_i (1-\gamma_i^{(j)}) X_i}{\sum_i (1-\gamma_i^{(j)})}.$$

Beautifully intuitive: each $\mu_k$ is a weighted mean of the data, where the weights are the soft labels from the E-step.

Key Takeaways

  • Fisher Information has two faces: variance of the score $\equiv$ negative expected curvature of the log-likelihood. The second form is often easier to compute.
  • Multivariate MLE is asymptotically normal with covariance matrix $I(\theta_0)^{-1}$ — the matrix generalization of $1/I(\theta_0)$.
  • The MLE works because it minimizes KL divergence asymptotically: by Jensen's inequality plus identifiability, the rescaled log-likelihood is uniquely maximized at the truth.
  • Method of Moments is a simpler alternative: match empirical moments to theoretical moments and solve. Consistent and asymptotically normal (via LLN, CMT, and delta method), but typically less efficient than MLE.
  • EM makes intractable likelihoods (like mixtures) tractable by introducing latent variables and iterating E-step / M-step. Each iteration monotonically increases the likelihood — but only guarantees convergence to a local maximum.
