Lecture 6 — Chi-Square Distribution, Pearson's Test & Likelihood Ratio Tests

Course: Core Concepts in Statistical Learning Lecture 6 Date: 2026-04-13
This lecture introduces three tools that belong together: the chi-square distribution, which will be the reference distribution for our test statistics; Pearson's $\chi^2$ test, which asks whether observed counts match an expected distribution; and likelihood ratio tests, a very general construction that compares how well two nested models fit the data. The common thread is that all three rely on squared deviations that, under the null hypothesis, behave asymptotically like sums of squared standard normals.

1. The Chi-Square Distribution

Before we can build tests, we need to know the distribution of the statistic we'll compare our data against. The chi-square distribution turns out to be the natural candidate whenever we sum up squared normal deviations — which happens all the time in statistics.

Definition — Chi-square distribution

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_m$ be i.i.d. random variables with $X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Then the random variable

$$V = \sum_{i=1}^m X_i^2 \sim \chi^2_{(m)}$$

is said to follow a chi-square distribution with $m$ degrees of freedom.

Why does this distribution show up?

The intuition is simple: whenever you have a statistic that — after standardization — behaves like a standard normal, then its square behaves like a $\chi^2_{(1)}$. If you have $m$ such independent standardized quantities and add their squares, you get $\chi^2_{(m)}$. Almost every asymptotic testing result in this lecture will boil down to recognizing this pattern.

Connection to the Gamma distribution

It can be shown that $\chi^2_{(m)}$ is in fact a special case of the Gamma distribution, specifically $\text{Gamma}(m/2, 1/2)$. Recall that $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ has density

$$f_{\alpha,\beta}(x) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta x} \mathbf{1}_{\{x>0\}}.$$

So a $\chi^2_{(m)}$ random variable $V$ has density

$$f_m(x) = \frac{1}{2^{m/2} \Gamma(m/2)} x^{m/2 - 1} e^{-x/2} \mathbf{1}_{\{x>0\}}.$$

Mean and variance

Using the Gamma formulas (mean $= \alpha/\beta$, variance $= \alpha/\beta^2$):

$$\mathbb{E}(V) = \frac{m/2}{1/2} = m, \qquad \mathbb{V}(V) = \frac{m/2}{(1/2)^2} = 2m.$$
Intuition check

The mean is $m$ because each $X_i^2$ contributes $\mathbb{E}(X_i^2) = \mathbb{V}(X_i) = 1$ on average. The variance grows linearly in $m$ because we're summing $m$ independent terms. Bigger $m$ $\Rightarrow$ the distribution shifts right and spreads out.

Example — $\chi^2_{(1)}$

If $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, then $Z^2 \sim \chi^2_{(1)}$. This will matter in a moment: squared $z$-statistics always become $\chi^2_{(1)}$ quantities. For instance, if your Wald statistic $W_n \overset{d}{\to} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, then $W_n^2 \overset{d}{\to} \chi^2_{(1)}$ by the Continuous Mapping Theorem.

2. Pearson's $\chi^2$ Test (Goodness-of-Fit)

Suppose a population is partitioned into $k$ categories $C_1, \ldots, C_k$ with probabilities $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ (so that $\sum_{j=1}^k p_j = 1$). We draw a random sample of $n$ individuals and want to ask: does the data agree with a hypothesized probability vector?

Setup

For each category $j \in \{1, \ldots, k\}$, let

$$N_j = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{\{\text{individual } i \in C_j\}}$$

be the count of individuals in category $C_j$. Note that $\sum_{j=1}^k N_j = n$ — this linear constraint means the counts cannot be mutually independent, and it will cost us one degree of freedom later.

The testing problem

Based on observed counts $(N_1, \ldots, N_k)$, we test

$$H_0: (p_1, \ldots, p_k) = (p_1^0, \ldots, p_k^0) \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1: (p_1, \ldots, p_k) \neq (p_1^0, \ldots, p_k^0)$$

where $p_1^0, \ldots, p_k^0$ are fixed probabilities with $\sum_{j=1}^k p_j^0 = 1$. This is called a goodness-of-fit test.

The test statistic

Under $H_0$, if category $j$ has probability $p_j^0$, then in a sample of size $n$ we'd expect about $n p_j^0$ individuals to land in $C_j$. The Pearson statistic measures how far the observed counts deviate from these expected counts, in a normalized way:

$$T_n = \sum_{j=1}^k \frac{(N_j - n p_j^0)^2}{n p_j^0}.$$

This is sometimes written heuristically as

$$T_n = \sum_j \frac{(O_j - E_j)^2}{E_j}$$

where $O_j$ stands for "observed" and $E_j = n p_j^0$ for "expected".

Why this specific form?

Each term $(N_j - n p_j^0)^2 / (n p_j^0)$ is essentially a squared $z$-score: the squared deviation divided by something proportional to the variance of $N_j$ (which is $n p_j^0 (1-p_j^0)$, approximately $n p_j^0$ when $p_j^0$ is small). Intuitively, we're summing $k$ squared, approximately standard-normal quantities — so we should expect something close to $\chi^2_{(k)}$ in the limit. But the constraint $\sum_j N_j = n$ removes one degree of freedom, giving us $\chi^2_{(k-1)}$.

Asymptotic distribution

It can be shown that under $H_0$:

$$T_n \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{(k-1)}.$$

Therefore, for a desired level $\alpha$, the test which

  • rejects $H_0$ if $T_n > q_{k-1, 1-\alpha}$,
  • does not reject $H_0$ otherwise,

where $q_{k-1, 1-\alpha}$ is the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of $\chi^2_{(k-1)}$, has asymptotic level $\alpha$.

Example — Mendel's peas

Mendel crossed peas with round yellow seeds and wrinkled green seeds and observed four types of progeny:

  1. Round yellow
  2. Wrinkled yellow
  3. Round green
  4. Wrinkled green

His theory predicted the probability vector

$$p^0 = \left( \tfrac{9}{16}, \tfrac{3}{16}, \tfrac{3}{16}, \tfrac{1}{16} \right).$$

In $n = 556$ experiments, he observed $(N_1, N_2, N_3, N_4) = (315, 101, 108, 32)$.

We want to test $H_0: p = p^0$ vs $H_1: p \neq p^0$ at level $\alpha = 0.05$.

Step 1 — Compute expected counts. With $n = 556$:

Category Observed $N_j$ Expected $np_j^0$
Round yellow315$556 \cdot 9/16 = 312.75$
Wrinkled yellow101$556 \cdot 3/16 = 104.25$
Round green108$556 \cdot 3/16 = 104.25$
Wrinkled green32$556 \cdot 1/16 = 34.75$

Step 2 — Compute the statistic.

$$T_n = \frac{(315 - 312.75)^2}{312.75} + \frac{(101 - 104.25)^2}{104.25} + \frac{(108 - 104.25)^2}{104.25} + \frac{(32 - 34.75)^2}{34.75} \approx 0.47.$$

Step 3 — Compare to the critical value. With $k - 1 = 3$ degrees of freedom, $q_{3, 0.95} \approx 7.815$.

Conclusion: Since $T_n \approx 0.47 \ll 7.815$, the test does not reject $H_0$. There is no evidence against Mendel's hypothesis — the observed counts are remarkably consistent with the $9{:}3{:}3{:}1$ ratio.

A rule of thumb

The $\chi^2$ approximation works well when all expected counts $n p_j^0$ are reasonably large (a common guideline is $\geq 5$). If some expected counts are very small, the test statistic's distribution may differ substantially from $\chi^2_{(k-1)}$, and you might prefer exact tests or combine sparse categories.

3. Likelihood Ratio Tests (LRT)

The Pearson test answers a specific question (do observed counts match expected probabilities?). The likelihood ratio test is a much more general recipe: given a parametric model, we compare how well the model fits when restricted to $H_0$ versus when it's free to use all of $\Theta$.

Setup

Consider a parametric model indexed by $\theta$. Let $\Theta_0$ and $\Theta_1$ be subsets of $\Theta$ with $\Theta_0 \cap \Theta_1 = \emptyset$ and $\Theta_0 \cup \Theta_1 = \Theta$. We test

$$H_0: \theta \in \Theta_0 \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1: \theta \in \Theta_1 = \Theta_0^c.$$
Likelihood ratio statistic

Let $L_n(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n f(X_i; \theta)$ be the likelihood. Define

$$\Lambda_n = \frac{\sup_{\theta \in \Theta} L_n(\theta)}{\sup_{\theta \in \Theta_0} L_n(\theta)} = \frac{L_n(\hat{\theta}_n)}{L_n(\hat{\theta}_n^0)}$$

where $\hat{\theta}_n$ is the unrestricted MLE and $\hat{\theta}_n^0$ is the MLE restricted to $\Theta_0$. The transformed statistic is

$$\lambda_n = 2 \log(\Lambda_n) = 2\big(\ell_n(\hat{\theta}_n) - \ell_n(\hat{\theta}_n^0)\big).$$

The intuition

Think of $\Lambda_n$ as a ratio of "best possible fits". The numerator is the likelihood when we can choose any $\theta$ in $\Theta$. The denominator is the likelihood when we're forced to stay inside $\Theta_0$.

  • If $H_0$ is true, the restriction to $\Theta_0$ doesn't hurt us much — the two maxima will be close, so $\Lambda_n \approx 1$ and $\lambda_n \approx 0$.
  • If $H_0$ is false, staying inside $\Theta_0$ does hurt — the unrestricted maximum is much higher, so $\Lambda_n$ grows and $\lambda_n$ becomes large.

So large $\lambda_n$ is evidence against $H_0$. The factor of 2 in $\lambda_n = 2\log(\Lambda_n)$ is not cosmetic — it's precisely what's needed for the limiting distribution to come out as a plain chi-square (no scaling).

Asymptotic distribution (Wilks' Theorem)

Under regularity conditions and under $H_0$:

$$\lambda_n \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{(m)}$$

where $m = \dim(\Theta) - \dim(\Theta_0)$ is the number of constraints imposed by $H_0$.

How to count constraints

If $\Theta = \mathbb{R}^k$ and for some $q \in \{0, \ldots, k-1\}$,

$$\Theta_0 = \{\theta \in \mathbb{R}^k : \theta_{q+1} = \theta_{q+1}^0, \ldots, \theta_k = \theta_k^0\},$$

then $H_0$ fixes the last $k - q$ coordinates, giving $m = k - q$ constraints.

Equivalently: $m$ is how many "free parameters you lose" by being forced into $\Theta_0$.

The test

Let $q_{m, 1-\alpha}$ be the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of $\chi^2_{(m)}$. The test that

  • rejects $H_0$ if $\lambda_n > q_{m, 1-\alpha}$,
  • does not reject $H_0$ otherwise,

has asymptotic level $\alpha$.

Example — Testing the mean of a Gaussian

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{i.i.d.}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1)$ with $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$. We test

$$H_0: \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1: \theta \neq \theta_0.$$

Here $\Theta = \mathbb{R}$ and $\Theta_0 = \{\theta_0\}$, so $m = 1 - 0 = 1$.

The unrestricted MLE is $\hat{\theta}_n = \bar{X}_n$ (we showed this in a previous lecture). Under $H_0$, the "restricted" MLE is trivially $\theta_0$ itself.

Computing $\lambda_n$. The log-likelihood is

$$\ell_n(\theta) = -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \theta)^2.$$

So

$$\lambda_n = 2\big(\ell_n(\bar{X}_n) - \ell_n(\theta_0)\big) = -\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2 + \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \theta_0)^2.$$

The algebraic trick. Expand the second sum by adding and subtracting $\bar{X}_n$:

$$\begin{aligned} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \theta_0)^2 &= \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n + \bar{X}_n - \theta_0)^2 \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2 + 2(\bar{X}_n - \theta_0)\underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)}_{=\,0} + n(\bar{X}_n - \theta_0)^2 \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2 + n(\bar{X}_n - \theta_0)^2. \end{aligned}$$

Substituting back:

$$\lambda_n = n(\bar{X}_n - \theta_0)^2 = \big(\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta_0)\big)^2.$$

Recognizing the distribution. Under $H_0$, $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta_0) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ exactly. So $\lambda_n = Z^2$ with $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, meaning

$$\lambda_n \sim \chi^2_{(1)} \quad \text{(exactly, not just asymptotically!).}$$

This confirms Wilks' theorem in a case where we can actually compute everything by hand — and better, the result here holds for finite $n$, so the LRT test has level $\alpha$ (exact, not just asymptotic).

Why the LRT is powerful

Notice how general this framework is. The LRT doesn't require you to guess a specific test statistic — you just compute the likelihood under both models and compare. This same recipe works for testing the mean of a normal, a regression coefficient being zero, one set of nested models vs another, whether certain categories can be combined, etc.

4. Testing Independence of Discrete Random Variables

We finish the lecture with an important application of the Pearson-style idea: testing whether two discrete variables are independent.

The 2×2 case

Let $Y$ and $Z$ be random variables defined on the same sample space, each taking two values. Without loss of generality, $Y(\Omega) = Z(\Omega) = \{0, 1\}$. Given $n$ i.i.d. pairs $(Y_1, Z_1), \ldots, (Y_n, Z_n)$ distributed like $(Y, Z)$, we want to test:

$$H_0: Y \perp\!\!\!\perp Z \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1: Y \not\perp\!\!\!\perp Z.$$

The contingency table

The data is summarized in a contingency table of counts:

$Z = 0$ $Z = 1$ Row total
$Y = 0$ $N_{00}$ $N_{01}$ $N_{0\cdot}$
$Y = 1$ $N_{10}$ $N_{11}$ $N_{1\cdot}$
Column total $N_{\cdot 0}$ $N_{\cdot 1}$ $n$

Here $N_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{\{Y_k = i, Z_k = j\}}$, row sums $N_{i\cdot} = \sum_j N_{ij}$, and column sums $N_{\cdot j} = \sum_i N_{ij}$.

At the population level, there's a matching table of probabilities $p_{ij} = \mathbb{P}(Y = i, Z = j)$, with marginals $p_{i\cdot}$ and $p_{\cdot j}$. The key observation:

$$Y \perp\!\!\!\perp Z \iff p_{ij} = p_{i\cdot} \, p_{\cdot j} \quad \forall i, j \in \{0, 1\}.$$

Building the test statistic

Under $H_0$, by the Law of Large Numbers, for large $n$ we'd expect

$$\frac{N_{ij}}{n} \approx \frac{N_{i\cdot}}{n} \cdot \frac{N_{\cdot j}}{n}, \quad \text{or equivalently,} \quad N_{ij} \approx \frac{N_{i\cdot} N_{\cdot j}}{n}.$$

The quantity $N_{i\cdot} N_{\cdot j} / n$ plays the role of the "expected" count under independence, now estimated from the data (using the observed marginals). This gives the Pearson $\chi^2$ statistic:

$$T_n = \sum_{i,j} \frac{\left( N_{ij} - \frac{N_{i\cdot} N_{\cdot j}}{n} \right)^2}{\frac{N_{i\cdot} N_{\cdot j}}{n}}.$$

It can be shown that under $H_0$:

$$T_n \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{(1)}.$$

The test that rejects $H_0$ if $T_n > q_{1, 1-\alpha}$ is of asymptotic level $\alpha$.

Why 1 degree of freedom?

In a 2×2 table, there are 4 cells, so naively you might expect $\chi^2_{(4)}$. But:

  • The total count is fixed at $n$ (−1 df).
  • Under $H_0$, we estimated $p_{0\cdot}$ (the row marginal — one parameter, since $p_{1\cdot} = 1 - p_{0\cdot}$) and $p_{\cdot 0}$ (the column marginal — one parameter). That's −2 df.

So $4 - 1 - 2 = 1$ degree of freedom. More generally, for an $r \times c$ table, the degrees of freedom are $(r-1)(c-1)$.

Example — Treatment and recovery

Suppose we want to know whether a new treatment ($Z$: 1 = treated, 0 = placebo) is associated with recovery ($Y$: 1 = recovered, 0 = not). Out of $n = 200$ patients, we observe:

Placebo ($Z = 0$) Treated ($Z = 1$) Total
Not recovered ($Y = 0$) 60 30 90
Recovered ($Y = 1$) 40 70 110
Total 100 100 200

Expected counts under $H_0$ (independence):

$$\frac{N_{i\cdot} N_{\cdot j}}{n}: \quad \frac{90 \cdot 100}{200} = 45, \quad \frac{110 \cdot 100}{200} = 55 \quad \text{(symmetric across columns)}.$$

So the expected counts are 45, 45, 55, 55 for the four cells. The statistic:

$$T_n = \frac{(60-45)^2}{45} + \frac{(30-45)^2}{45} + \frac{(40-55)^2}{55} + \frac{(70-55)^2}{55} = 5 + 5 + \tfrac{225}{55} + \tfrac{225}{55} \approx 18.18.$$

Comparing to $q_{1, 0.95} \approx 3.841$: since $18.18 \gg 3.841$, we reject $H_0$. There is strong evidence that recovery depends on treatment.

5. Key Takeaways

  • The $\chi^2$ distribution arises as the distribution of a sum of squared independent standard normals. Its degrees of freedom count how many such "free" squared normals you're summing. It has mean $m$ and variance $2m$.
  • Pearson's $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test compares observed counts to expected counts via $T_n = \sum_j (N_j - np_j^0)^2/(np_j^0)$. Under $H_0$, $T_n \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{(k-1)}$ — we lose one df for the constraint $\sum N_j = n$.
  • The Likelihood Ratio Test is a general-purpose recipe: compare the best fit over all of $\Theta$ to the best fit inside $\Theta_0$ using $\lambda_n = 2(\ell_n(\hat{\theta}_n) - \ell_n(\hat{\theta}_n^0))$. Large $\lambda_n$ is evidence against $H_0$.
  • Wilks' theorem: under regularity conditions, $\lambda_n \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{(m)}$ where $m = \dim(\Theta) - \dim(\Theta_0)$ counts the constraints $H_0$ imposes.
  • Independence testing in contingency tables uses the same Pearson statistic, but the "expected" counts $N_{i\cdot} N_{\cdot j}/n$ are now estimated from the data. In a 2×2 table, the limiting distribution is $\chi^2_{(1)}$; more generally, $(r-1)(c-1)$ for $r \times c$ tables.
  • A unifying theme: all these tests convert deviations from $H_0$ into squared, standardized quantities that accumulate into a $\chi^2$ under the null. Large statistic values are unlikely under $H_0$, so they're what we reject on.
