Lecture 9 – Stability Selection & Multi Sample Splitting

Course: Statistics for High-Dimensional Data Lecture 9 Based on Bühlmann & van de Geer (2011), Ch. 10–11

This lecture tackles the problem of quantifying uncertainty in high-dimensional variable selection: given that the Lasso picks a handful of variables from thousands of candidates, which of them are we actually confident about? Because the usual statistical tools — bootstrap, asymptotic normality — fail for the Lasso, we need different strategies. Two generic resampling-based approaches are introduced: Stability Selection, which turns subsampling into a way to control the expected number of false positives, and Multi Sample Splitting, which combines p-values from many half-sample splits to produce valid p-values for high-dimensional linear models.

1. Why Inference on the Lasso Is Hard

The Lasso gives us a point estimate $\hat\beta(\lambda)$, but it does not naturally come with confidence intervals or p-values. The obvious idea — bootstrap the Lasso — doesn't work.

Why the Lasso Bootstrap Fails

The Lasso has a non-Gaussian limiting distribution, especially near zero where soft-thresholding kicks in. Formally, if

$$ a_n\bigl(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{Lasso};j} - \beta_j^0\bigr) \Longrightarrow Z, \qquad Z \sim F \text{ (non-Gaussian)}, $$

then the bootstrap analogue does not converge to the same distribution:

$$ a_n\bigl(\hat\beta^{*}_{\mathrm{Lasso};j} - \hat\beta_{\mathrm{Lasso};j}\bigr) \not\Longrightarrow Z \quad \text{in probability.} $$

Bootstrap consistency requires sufficient smoothness of the estimator; soft-thresholding is non-smooth exactly at zero, which is where much of the action is in a sparse model.

1.1 Two Paths Forward

There are (at least) two routes around this problem:

  • De-biased / de-sparsified Lasso (previous lectures): remove the shrinkage bias analytically so a central limit theorem kicks in, giving asymptotic normality and classical confidence intervals. Statistically optimal but requires more machinery.
  • Resampling-based approaches (this lecture): use subsampling or sample splitting to bypass the Lasso's pathological distribution entirely. Less statistically efficient but extremely generic — they work for almost any selector, not just the Lasso.
Intuition

Think of the Lasso as a machine that picks variables. The question we're asking is: how do I trust what came out? Option 1 (de-biasing) opens the machine and fixes its internal bias. Option 2 (resampling) doesn't touch the machine — instead, we feed it many slightly different datasets and see which variables it keeps picking. The second option is the spirit of Chapter 10 and 11.

2. Stability Selection

Stability Selection (Meinshausen & Bühlmann, 2010) starts from a very simple observation: if a variable is truly relevant, it should be selected consistently across perturbations of the data; noise variables, on the other hand, will be picked by pure luck and rarely repeat.

2.1 Setup and Subsampling Probability

Setup

We have i.i.d. data $Z_1, \ldots, Z_n$ (main example: $Z_i = (X_i, Y_i)$ for regression), and a generic selector $\hat S_\lambda \subseteq \{1, \ldots, p\}$ that depends on a regularization parameter $\lambda$. The prime example is the Lasso, where $\hat S(\lambda) = \{j : \hat\beta_j(\lambda) \neq 0\}$.

Let $I^*$ be a random subsample of $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$ observations, drawn without replacement. For each variable $j$, we define its selection probability under subsampling:

$$ \hat\Pi_j(\lambda) \;=\; \mathbb{P}^*\bigl[\, j \in \hat S_\lambda(I^*) \,\bigr], $$

where $\mathbb{P}^*$ is probability over the random subsample. In practice we approximate this by running $B$ subsamples and counting:

$$ \hat\Pi_j(\lambda) \;\approx\; \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} \mathbb{1}\bigl\{ j \in \hat S_\lambda(I^{*b}) \bigr\}. $$

2.2 Stable Variables

Set of Stable Variables

For a threshold $\pi_{\mathrm{thr}} \in (0, 1)$ and a set $\Lambda$ of regularization values,

$$ \hat S_{\mathrm{stable}} \;=\; \Bigl\{\, j \;:\; \max_{\lambda \in \Lambda} \hat\Pi_j(\lambda) \;\geq\; \pi_{\mathrm{thr}} \,\Bigr\}. $$

We keep variables selected in a large fraction of subsamples and disregard the rest.

Toy Example

Suppose we run the Lasso on $B = 100$ subsamples with $p = 1000$ candidate variables. Say gene $X_{17}$ appears in 92 of the 100 fitted models, while gene $X_{253}$ appears in 4. With threshold $\pi_{\mathrm{thr}} = 0.6$, we declare $X_{17}$ stable (relevant) and $X_{253}$ unstable (noise). The magic is that we did not need to pick a single "correct" $\lambda$ — we just took the maximum over $\lambda \in \Lambda$.

2.3 Error Control

Choosing $\pi_{\mathrm{thr}}$ well matters — how do we do it? The main theorem of Meinshausen & Bühlmann gives a bound on the expected number of false positives. Let $q_\Lambda = \mathbb{E}\bigl|\bigcup_{\lambda \in \Lambda} \hat S(\lambda)\bigr|$ be the expected number of variables ever selected across $\Lambda$, and let $V$ be the number of false positives in $\hat S_{\mathrm{stable}}$.

Theorem 10.1 (Error control, informal)

Under an exchangeability assumption on noise-variable selection and mild non-triviality, for $\pi_{\mathrm{thr}} \in (1/2, 1)$:

$$ \mathbb{E}[V] \;\leq\; \frac{1}{2\pi_{\mathrm{thr}} - 1} \cdot \frac{q_\Lambda^2}{p}. $$

This is wonderfully useful in practice. If we want $\mathbb{E}[V] \leq \nu$ (a pre-specified error budget — say $\nu = 2.5$ false positives allowed), we can solve for the threshold:

$$ \pi_{\mathrm{thr}} \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\!\left(1 + \frac{q_\Lambda^2}{p\,\nu}\right). $$
Worked Calculation

With $p = 1000$ covariates, an expected number of selected variables of $q_\Lambda = 30$ ("top 30" entering the regularization path), and an error budget of $\nu = 5$ expected false positives, the threshold is

$$ \pi_{\mathrm{thr}} \;=\; \frac{1}{2} + \frac{30^2}{2 \cdot 1000 \cdot 5} \;=\; 0.5 + 0.09 \;=\; 0.59. $$

So we keep every variable selected in at least 59% of subsamples.

Why $\pi_{\mathrm{thr}} > 1/2$?

Looking at the formula: if $\pi_{\mathrm{thr}} \leq 1/2$ the denominator $2\pi_{\mathrm{thr}} - 1$ becomes $\leq 0$ and the bound breaks down. Intuitively, a variable "selected more than half the time" is meaningful; "selected less than half the time" is essentially uninformative.

2.4 Practical Recommendations

  • Don't agonize over $\lambda$. Take $|\hat S_\lambda|$ fairly large (loose regularization) and let stability selection do the pruning for you.
  • Results are robust to $\Lambda$. Empirically, as long as $\Lambda$ contains values leading to overselection of $S_0$, the stability-selected set barely changes.
  • It's very generic. The method works for any selector: Lasso, adaptive Lasso, boosting, forward selection, GLasso for graphical models, etc.
Caveat on Assumptions

Theorem 10.1 relies on an exchangeability assumption on noise variables that is restrictive and rarely holds exactly. In practice, though, the method usually works very well — simulations show error control holds beyond the strict theoretical conditions. As the slides summarize: "the sufficient assumptions (far from necessary) are restrictive, but the method seems to work very well in practice."

2.5 Extension: Randomized Lasso

An interesting refinement is to add random perturbations to the penalty. The Randomized Lasso uses weighted $\ell_1$-norm with random weights:

$$ \hat\beta_{\mathrm{random}}(\lambda) \;=\; \arg\min_\beta \left( \|Y - X\beta\|_2^2 / n + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^{p} W_j |\beta_j| \right), $$

where the weights $W_j$ are drawn i.i.d. from a two-point distribution $\{\gamma, 1\}$ with $\gamma \in (0.2, 0.8)$ (the "weakness" parameter). Applied many times in combination with stability selection, this further improves variable selection and can weaken the assumptions needed for consistency — from the irrepresentable condition down to a sparse eigenvalue condition.

3. From Stability Selection to P-values

Stability Selection controls the expected number of false positives, $\mathbb{E}[V]$. In many applications we want stronger error control — specifically, the familywise error rate:

$$ \mathrm{FWER} \;=\; \mathbb{P}[V > 0] \;=\; \text{probability of at least one false selection}. $$

To get real p-values with FWER (or FDR) control, we turn to a different resampling idea: sample splitting.

4. Single Sample Splitting (Wasserman & Roeder)

The idea, proposed by Wasserman & Roeder (2009), is disarmingly simple. Use one half of the data to select a manageable subset of variables; then use the other half — which is independent — to run classical inference on the selected subset.

4.1 The Procedure

  1. Split the data randomly into two disjoint halves $I_1, I_2 \subset \{1, \ldots, n\}$ of equal size $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$.
  2. Select on $I_1$: run Lasso (or any screening procedure) to get $\hat S(I_1) \subseteq \{1, \ldots, p\}$.
  3. Test on $I_2$: fit ordinary least squares using only the variables in $\hat S(I_1)$ on the data $(X_{I_2}, Y_{I_2})$, and run standard t-tests.

For each variable $j$, the raw p-value is defined as

$$ \tilde P_j \;=\; \begin{cases} P_{\mathrm{raw},j} \text{ from t-test on } Y_{I_2},\, X_{I_2}^{(\hat S(I_1))}, & \text{if } j \in \hat S(I_1), \\ 1, & \text{if } j \notin \hat S(I_1). \end{cases} $$

Variables not even selected get p-value 1 (we assume they're null). The crucial subtlety: because $I_1$ and $I_2$ are independent, the selection step doesn't invalidate the t-test. This sidesteps the "selective inference" problem.

4.2 Why It's a Valid P-value

Validity of single-split p-values

Testing $H_{0,j}: \beta_j^0 = 0$ versus $H_{A,j}: \beta_j^0 \neq 0$, under the fixed-design linear model with Gaussian errors:

  • If the screening property $\hat S(I_1) \supseteq S_0$ holds, then $P_{\mathrm{raw},j}$ is a valid p-value for $H_{0,j}$ (type-I error control).
  • If the screening property fails, $P_{\mathrm{raw},j}$ is still a valid p-value — but for the projected hypothesis $H_{0,j}(M): \beta_j(M) = 0$ where $M = \hat S(I_1)$ is the sub-model and $\beta(M) = ((X^{(M)})^T X^{(M)})^{-1} (X^{(M)})^T Y$ is the OLS coefficient within that sub-model.

4.3 Multiplicity Correction

Since we can have at most $|\hat S(I_1)|$ false positives (non-selected variables automatically get p-value 1), we can use Bonferroni with factor $|\hat S(I_1)|$ rather than with factor $p$ — a much tighter correction:

$$ \tilde P_{\mathrm{corr},j} \;=\; \min\bigl(\tilde P_j \cdot |\hat S(I_1)|,\; 1\bigr), \qquad j = 1, \ldots, p. $$

Rejecting whenever $\tilde P_{\mathrm{corr},j} \leq \alpha$ yields $\mathrm{FWER} \leq \alpha$ (asymptotically).

Numerical Example

Suppose $p = 1000$ and the Lasso on $I_1$ selects 20 variables. A variable $j$ in $\hat S(I_1)$ has raw t-test p-value $P_{\mathrm{raw},j} = 0.002$. Naïve Bonferroni over all $p$ would give $0.002 \times 1000 = 2.0 \to 1$ — not significant. Using the sample-splitting correction, we only multiply by 20: $\tilde P_{\mathrm{corr},j} = 0.002 \times 20 = 0.04$ — significant at $\alpha = 0.05$. The screening step gives us a power gain because we didn't have to pay for testing all $p$ hypotheses.

5. The "P-value Lottery" Problem

The single-split method is elegant, but has a serious flaw: the result depends drastically on the random split. Different splits give wildly different p-values for the same variable.

This is clearly unsatisfactory — scientific conclusions shouldn't flip based on a random seed. The natural fix is to average over many splits.

6. Multi Sample Splitting

Proposed by Meinshausen, Meier & Bühlmann (2009), the idea is to repeat the sample-splitting procedure $B$ times and aggregate the resulting p-values in a principled way.

6.1 Procedure

Multi Sample Split Algorithm

For $b = 1, \ldots, B$:

  1. Randomly split the data into two disjoint halves $I_1^{[b]}$ and $I_2^{[b]}$.
  2. On $I_1^{[b]}$: estimate $\hat S^{[b]} = \hat S(I_1^{[b]})$ (e.g., Lasso).
  3. On $I_2^{[b]}$: compute adjusted p-values $\tilde P_{\mathrm{corr},j}^{[b]} = \min(\tilde P_j^{[b]} \cdot |\hat S^{[b]}|,\, 1)$.

Then aggregate $\tilde P_{\mathrm{corr},j}^{[1]}, \ldots, \tilde P_{\mathrm{corr},j}^{[B]}$ into a single p-value for each $j$.

6.2 The Aggregation Problem

The p-values from different splits are dependent (the splits share observations), so we can't simply use Fisher's method or Stouffer's method. Instead, Meinshausen et al. use a quantile-based aggregation. For $\gamma \in (0, 1)$:

$$ Q_j(\gamma) \;=\; \min\!\left\{\, q_\gamma\!\bigl(\bigl\{ \tilde P_{\mathrm{corr},j}^{[b]} / \gamma \;:\; b = 1, \ldots, B \bigr\}\bigr),\; 1 \,\right\}, $$

where $q_\gamma(\cdot)$ denotes the empirical $\gamma$-quantile. For any fixed $\gamma$, $Q_j(\gamma)$ is an asymptotically valid FWER-controlling p-value.

Why dividing by $\gamma$?

The division by $\gamma$ compensates for taking a quantile: if we take the 25th percentile (small $\gamma$) we're looking at small p-values, which would naturally be too optimistic without a correction. The factor $1/\gamma$ restores conservativeness.

6.3 Adaptive Aggregation

Picking a single $\gamma$ is arbitrary. An adaptive version optimizes over $\gamma$ at a small cost:

$$ P_j \;=\; \min\!\left\{ (1 - \log \gamma_{\min})\, \inf_{\gamma \in (\gamma_{\min},\, 1)} Q_j(\gamma),\; 1 \right\}. $$

With the recommended $\gamma_{\min} = 0.05$, the correction factor is $1 - \log(0.05) \approx 3.996$, a modest price for being allowed to search over $\gamma$.

6.4 Main Theorem

Theorem 11.1 (FWER control for multi sample split)

Consider the fixed-design linear model $Y = X\beta + \varepsilon$ with Gaussian errors, and assume the screening property $\mathbb{P}[\hat S_{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} \supseteq S_0] \to 1$, a sparsity bound $\mathbb{P}[|\hat S_{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}| < n/2] \to 1$, and a mild design condition. With a fixed number $B$ of random splits and $\gamma_{\min} \in (0, 1)$:

$$ \limsup_{n \to \infty} \mathbb{P}\bigl[V_{\mathrm{multi-split\;FWER}}(\alpha) > 0\bigr] \;\leq\; \alpha, $$

where $\hat S_{\mathrm{multi-split\;FWER}}(\alpha) = \{j : P_j \leq \alpha\}$.

6.5 FDR Control

If FWER is too conservative, the same $P_j$'s can be fed into a Benjamini–Hochberg-type procedure for False Discovery Rate control. Since these p-values are already multiplicity-corrected at the sub-model level, we don't need the usual factor of $p$ in BH:

$$ h(q) \;=\; \max\bigl\{\, i \;:\; P_{(i)} \leq i \cdot q \,\bigr\}, \qquad \hat S_{\mathrm{multi-split\;FDR}}(q) = \{ j : P_j \leq P_{(h(q))}\}. $$

For FDR control at level $\alpha$, choose $q(\alpha) = \alpha / \sum_{i=1}^{p} i^{-1}$ (the extra sum comes from Benjamini–Yekutieli for dependent p-values).

7. Comparing Stability Selection and Multi Sample Splitting

Aspect Stability Selection Multi Sample Splitting
Resampling Subsampling (size $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$), $B$ times Random split into two halves, $B$ times
Output Set of "stable" variables P-value $P_j$ for each variable
Error controlled $\mathbb{E}[V]$ (expected # false positives) FWER or FDR
Key assumption Exchangeability (restrictive) Screening property + sparsity
Generality Very generic — any selector, any model Generic; needs valid low-dimensional test on $I_2$
Multiplicity correction Implicit via $\pi_{\mathrm{thr}}$ and $q_\Lambda$ Bonferroni factor $|\hat S(I_1)|$, not $p$
Which to Use When?

Use stability selection when you want a shortlist of reliably-selected variables and don't need formal p-values — or when you're using a non-standard selector (e.g., GLasso, boosting) where building a proper test is hard. Use multi sample splitting when you need formal p-values with FWER or FDR control, especially for downstream decisions or publication. Both can be combined with refitting (e.g., OLS on the selected variables) for prediction.

8. Practical Notes and Software

  • R package hdi (Meier et al.) implements multi sample splitting, stability selection, de-sparsified Lasso, and bootstrap-based variants.
  • Multiple-testing correction on the resulting p-values: use p.adjust in R with method "holm" (Bonferroni–Holm) for FWER or "BH" (Benjamini–Hochberg) for FDR.
  • Recent developments (Schultheiss et al., 2021): "multi carve" methods combine sample splitting with selective inference for additional power.

Key Takeaways

  • Standard Lasso inference is broken. The Lasso bootstrap is inconsistent because the Lasso has a non-Gaussian limiting distribution — especially around zero where soft-thresholding creates non-smoothness. We need different tools for p-values and confidence intervals.
  • Stability Selection uses subsampling to identify reliably-selected variables. A variable is kept if it appears in the selected set of at least a fraction $\pi_{\mathrm{thr}}$ of random $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$-subsamples. Theorem 10.1 gives a tunable bound on the expected number of false positives, at the cost of an exchangeability assumption that is often unrealistic but rarely fatal in practice.
  • Single sample splitting exploits independence. Split the data; select on $I_1$; test classically (t-tests) on $I_2$. Because the two halves are independent, the selection step does not invalidate the test — a rare clean solution to post-selection inference. Multiplicity correction uses Bonferroni with factor $|\hat S(I_1)|$, much tighter than factor $p$.
  • The p-value lottery motivates multi sample splitting. A single split's p-value varies wildly across splits. Repeating $B$ times and aggregating via quantile-based statistics yields reproducible, asymptotically valid p-values — the recipe is $Q_j(\gamma) = q_\gamma(\{\tilde P^{[b]}_{\mathrm{corr},j}/\gamma\})$, with an adaptive correction $(1 - \log \gamma_{\min})$ for searching over $\gamma$.
  • Both methods are extremely generic. They work with any reasonable selector and any model where downstream inference is feasible on $I_2$ — GLMs, additive models, graphical models, not just linear regression. This genericity is often more valuable than the statistical optimality of the de-sparsified Lasso.
  • Choose the method by the error measure you need. Stability selection controls $\mathbb{E}[V]$ (per-family error rate). Multi sample splitting controls FWER or FDR. They are complementary, not competing — Meinshausen–Bühlmann gives a variable list; Meinshausen–Meier–Bühlmann gives inferential p-values.
