Testing Independence, p-values, and Multiple Testing

Course: Core Concepts in Statistical Learning Lecture 7 Spring 2026

This lecture extends independence testing for discrete random variables to the general $I \times J$ contingency table, revisits the same problem through the lens of the likelihood ratio test, and then turns to two ideas that shape how we report and combine statistical tests in practice: the p-value, a continuous summary of how extreme an observation is under $H_0$, and multiple testing corrections, which address the fact that running many tests at level $\alpha$ inflates the chance of at least one false rejection. A central theme throughout is that a "level-$\alpha$ guarantee" is a statement about one test under one null — once we ask more questions, we must adjust.

1. Testing Independence in the General Case

In Lecture 6 we tested independence of two binary random variables using a $2\times 2$ contingency table. Now we generalise: $Y$ takes values in $\{1,\dots,I\}$ and $Z$ in $\{1,\dots,J\}$, so each individual falls into one of $I\cdot J$ cells.

1.1 Setup and Notation

We observe i.i.d. pairs $(Y_k, Z_k)$, $k=1,\dots,n$, and count how many fall into each cell. The data is organised in an $I \times J$ contingency table.

Definition — Cell and marginal counts

For $1 \le i \le I$, $1 \le j \le J$, define

$$ N_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \mathbf{1}\{Y_k=i,\, Z_k=j\}, \quad N_{i\cdot} = \sum_{j=1}^{J} N_{ij}, \quad N_{\cdot j} = \sum_{i=1}^{I} N_{ij}. $$

$N_{ij}$ is the cell count, $N_{i\cdot}$ the row marginal, $N_{\cdot j}$ the column marginal, and $\sum_{i,j} N_{ij} = n$.

On the population side, we have true probabilities $p_{ij} = P(Y=i, Z=j)$ with row and column marginals $p_{i\cdot} = P(Y=i)$ and $p_{\cdot j} = P(Z=j)$. The testing problem is

$$ H_0: Y \perp\!\!\!\perp Z \quad \text{versus} \quad H_1: Y \not\perp\!\!\!\perp Z. $$

Recall the key equivalence: independence means the joint factorises into marginals, $p_{ij} = p_{i\cdot}\, p_{\cdot j}$ for all $(i,j)$.

1.2 The Intuition Behind the Test Statistic

Under $H_0$, the Weak Law of Large Numbers suggests that empirical frequencies converge to probabilities:

$$ \frac{N_{ij}}{n} \approx p_{ij} = p_{i\cdot}\, p_{\cdot j} \approx \frac{N_{i\cdot}}{n} \cdot \frac{N_{\cdot j}}{n}. $$

Multiplying by $n$, we get the expected count under independence:

$$ E_{ij} := \frac{N_{i\cdot}\, N_{\cdot j}}{n}. $$
Intuition — "observed vs expected under independence"

If $Y$ and $Z$ are truly independent, knowing the row totals and column totals should essentially let us predict the cell counts — each cell count should be roughly "row share × column share × total". The Pearson statistic measures how far off we actually are, cell by cell.

Definition — Pearson $\chi^2$ statistic for independence
$$ T_n = \sum_{i=1}^{I} \sum_{j=1}^{J} \frac{\bigl(N_{ij} - N_{i\cdot} N_{\cdot j}/n\bigr)^2}{N_{i\cdot} N_{\cdot j}/n}. $$

Under $H_0$, it can be shown that

$$ T_n \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{(I-1)(J-1)}. $$

So the test at asymptotic level $\alpha$ rejects $H_0$ whenever $T_n > q_{(I-1)(J-1),\,1-\alpha}$, the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of the $\chi^2_{(I-1)(J-1)}$ distribution, and does not reject otherwise.

Example — Streaming platform: device × genre preference

Suppose a streaming service wants to know whether the device a user watches on ($I = 3$: phone, laptop, TV) is independent of their favourite genre ($J = 4$: comedy, drama, documentary, action). Collecting $n = 1000$ users gives a $3 \times 4$ table of counts.

For each cell, compute the expected count under independence, e.g. if $600$ users watch on phones and $250$ prefer comedy, the expected count for (phone, comedy) is $600 \cdot 250 / 1000 = 150$. Compute the Pearson statistic by summing $(N_{ij} - E_{ij})^2 / E_{ij}$ across all $12$ cells, and compare it to the quantile of $\chi^2_{(3-1)(4-1)} = \chi^2_6$. If the statistic exceeds $q_{6, 0.95} \approx 12.59$, we reject independence at the $5\%$ level — meaning device choice carries real information about genre preference.

1.3 Why $(I-1)(J-1)$ Degrees of Freedom?

The degrees of freedom come from a dimension-counting argument. Intuitively: the $\chi^2$ distribution's degrees of freedom equal the number of free parameters in the full model minus the number of free parameters under the null.

Definition — The simplex $S_k$

The $k$-dimensional probability simplex is

$$ S_k = \Bigl\{ (s_1, \dots, s_k) : 0 \le s_i \le 1,\, \sum_{i=1}^{k} s_i = 1 \Bigr\}. $$

It has dimension $\dim(S_k) = k - 1$ (one constraint removes one degree of freedom).

Now:

  • Full model $\Theta$: all joint distributions $(p_{ij})$ on $I \times J$ cells summing to $1$. This is $S_{IJ}$, so $\dim(\Theta) = IJ - 1$.
  • Null model $\Theta_0$: all distributions of the form $p_{ij} = p_{i\cdot}\, p_{\cdot j}$. The map $((p_{i\cdot}), (p_{\cdot j})) \mapsto (p_{i\cdot}\, p_{\cdot j})$ is a bijection between $S_I \times S_J$ and $\Theta_0$, so $\dim(\Theta_0) = (I-1) + (J-1) = I + J - 2$.

The degrees of freedom are the difference:

$$ \dim(\Theta) - \dim(\Theta_0) = (IJ - 1) - (I + J - 2) = (I-1)(J-1). $$
Quick check — the $2\times 2$ case

For $I = J = 2$, we get $(2-1)(2-1) = 1$ degree of freedom, matching what we saw in Lecture 6. Intuitively, in a $2 \times 2$ table, once the two row totals and two column totals are fixed, only one cell count is free to vary — the other three are determined by the marginals.

2. Testing Independence via Likelihood Ratio

Pearson's statistic comes from a geometric "observed vs. expected" intuition. The likelihood ratio test (LRT) arrives at essentially the same answer from a completely different starting point: comparing the fit of the best model under $H_0$ with the fit of the best model overall.

2.1 The Multinomial Likelihood

The vector of counts $N = (N_{ij})$ has a multinomial distribution with parameters $n$ and $p = (p_{ij})$:

$$ L_n(p) = \frac{n!}{\prod_{i,j} N_{ij}!} \prod_{i=1}^{I} \prod_{j=1}^{J} p_{ij}^{N_{ij}}. $$

The LRT compares the likelihood under the best unconstrained estimate with the likelihood under the best independence-constrained estimate:

$$ \Lambda_n = \frac{\sup_{p \in \Theta} L_n(p)}{\sup_{p \in \Theta_0} L_n(p)} = \frac{L_n(\hat p_n)}{L_n(\hat p_n^0)}. $$

The two MLEs have clean forms:

$$ \hat p_n = \left(\frac{N_{ij}}{n}\right)_{i,j}, \qquad \hat p_n^0 = \left(\frac{N_{i\cdot}}{n} \cdot \frac{N_{\cdot j}}{n}\right)_{i,j}. $$
Intuition — what each MLE captures

$\hat p_n$ just takes each empirical frequency at face value — "whatever you saw is my best guess." $\hat p_n^0$ forces the independence structure and only uses the marginals — it ignores correlations and rebuilds the joint from the margins. The ratio asks: how much better does the unconstrained estimate fit the data than the independence-constrained one? If the answer is "barely," independence is a fine story.

2.2 The Log-Likelihood Ratio Statistic

Taking logs and multiplying by 2 gives

$$ \lambda_n = 2 \log \Lambda_n = 2 \sum_{i,j} N_{ij} \log\!\left( \frac{N_{ij}\, n}{N_{i\cdot}\, N_{\cdot j}} \right). $$

Like the Pearson statistic, under $H_0$:

$$ \lambda_n \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{(I-1)(J-1)}. $$

The test rejects $H_0$ if $\lambda_n > q_{(I-1)(J-1),\,1-\alpha}$. In practice the Pearson and LRT statistics often give very similar numerical values and always the same answer asymptotically; they are different first-order approximations to the same underlying log-likelihood difference.

Example — side-by-side in a toy table

Consider a $2\times 2$ table with counts:

$Z = 0$$Z = 1$Row total

Expected counts under independence: $E_{00}=35$, $E_{01}=65$, $E_{10}=35$, $E_{11}=65$. Pearson statistic: $T_n = \frac{25}{35} + \frac{25}{65} + \frac{25}{35} + \frac{25}{65} \approx 2.20$. The LRT statistic comes out nearby, around $\lambda_n \approx 2.20$. Both are compared against $q_{1, 0.95} \approx 3.84$, so neither rejects at the $5\%$ level. The two statistics tell the same story.

3. p-values

3.1 Why We Need More Than a Yes/No Answer

A level-$\alpha$ test produces a binary verdict: reject or don't reject. But that throws away information. Consider testing $H_0: \theta = 0$ for $X_1, \dots, X_{100} \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1)$ with $\alpha = 0.05$. The rejection threshold is $|\bar X_n| > z_{0.975}/\sqrt{n} \approx 0.196$. Both observations below exceed the threshold and both lead to "reject":

  • $\bar X_n = 0.21$ — barely over the line.
  • $\bar X_n = -5.17$ — astronomically far away.

Clearly $-5.17$ gives overwhelmingly stronger evidence against $H_0$ than $0.21$, but the binary verdict hides this. The p-value restores the missing information.

3.2 Defining the p-value

Definition — p-value

Suppose for each level $\alpha \in (0,1)$ we have a test with rejection region $R_\alpha$. The p-value at observation $(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ is

$$ p(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \inf\{\alpha \in (0,1) : (x_1, \dots, x_n) \in R_\alpha\}. $$

In words: the p-value is the smallest level at which the test would reject $H_0$ given the data.

Intuition — the "how close to the edge" reading

Imagine slowly lowering $\alpha$ from $1$ toward $0$. At high $\alpha$, the rejection region is huge and almost any data rejects. As $\alpha$ shrinks, $R_\alpha$ contracts. The p-value is the exact $\alpha$ at which your data "falls out" of the rejection region. A tiny p-value means the data keeps rejecting even at very strict levels — strong evidence against $H_0$. A large p-value means the data stops rejecting quickly — weak evidence.

3.3 The Tail-Probability Formula

In the practically important case where the rejection region has the form

$$ R_\alpha = \{(x_1, \dots, x_n) : T(x_1, \dots, x_n) > c_\alpha\} $$

for a test statistic $T$ and thresholds $c_\alpha$, we can derive a much more useful expression. Under mild regularity assumptions (there is a worst-case $\theta_0 \in \Theta_0$, and the CDF $F_{\theta_0}$ of $T$ is strictly increasing and continuous), the p-value equals

$$ p(x_1, \dots, x_n) = P_{\theta_0}\!\bigl( T(X_1, \dots, X_n) > T(x_1, \dots, x_n) \bigr) = \sup_{\theta \in \Theta_0} P_\theta\!\bigl( T(X_1, \dots, X_n) > T(x_1, \dots, x_n) \bigr). $$
Intuition — "how surprising is this under $H_0$?"

The p-value is the probability, if $H_0$ were true, of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as what we actually saw. Small p-value means "this would almost never happen under $H_0$" — so the null looks implausible.

Proof sketch

Starting from the definition, and writing $c_\alpha = F_{\theta_0}^{-1}(1-\alpha)$:

$$ \begin{aligned} p(x) &= \inf\{\alpha : T(x) > F_{\theta_0}^{-1}(1-\alpha)\} \\ &= \inf\{\alpha : F_{\theta_0}(T(x)) > 1 - \alpha\} \\ &= \inf\{\alpha : \alpha > 1 - F_{\theta_0}(T(x))\} \\ &= 1 - F_{\theta_0}(T(x)) \\ &= P_{\theta_0}(T(X_1,\dots,X_n) > T(x)). \end{aligned} $$

The worst-case choice of $\theta_0$ takes the sup over all $\theta \in \Theta_0$.

3.4 Worked Example: One-Sided Gaussian Test

Example — $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1)$, testing $H_0: \theta \le \theta_0$

The level-$\alpha$ test rejects $H_0$ when $\sqrt{n}(\bar X_n - \theta_0) > z_{1-\alpha}$, so $T(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \sqrt{n}(\bar x_n - \theta_0)$.

For $\theta \le \theta_0$, the CDF of $T$ satisfies $F_\theta(t) \ge F_{\theta_0}(t)$ (because adding a non-positive shift makes the statistic smaller in distribution), so $\theta_0$ is the worst case. Therefore

$$ p(x_1,\dots,x_n) = 1 - \Phi\!\bigl(\sqrt{n}(\bar x_n - \theta_0)\bigr), $$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. Plugging in $n = 100$, $\theta_0 = 0$:

Observed $\bar x_n$$\sqrt{n}\,\bar x_n$p-valueInterpretation
$0.03$$0.3$$\approx 0.38$Very weak evidence against $H_0$
$0.18$$1.8$$\approx 0.03$Moderate evidence against $H_0$
$0.82$$8.2$$\approx 1.1 \times 10^{-16}$Overwhelming evidence against $H_0$

Notice how the p-value smoothly tracks how extreme the observation is, whereas a fixed-$\alpha$ test would only give you "reject" or "don't reject."

Warning — common p-value misinterpretations
  • A p-value is not the probability that $H_0$ is true. It's a probability computed assuming $H_0$ is true.
  • A large p-value does not prove $H_0$ — it only says the data are compatible with $H_0$.
  • p-values depend on the test statistic. Two different tests on the same data can give different p-values.
  • p-values are themselves random (computed from $X_1,\dots,X_n$). Under $H_0$ with a continuous test statistic, the p-value is $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$.

4. Multiple Testing and the Bonferroni Correction

4.1 The Problem: Many Tests Inflate Errors

Now suppose we have $m$ separate testing problems:

$$ H_0^{(j)} : \theta \in \Theta_0^{(j)} \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1^{(j)} : \theta \in \Theta_1^{(j)}, \qquad j = 1, \dots, m. $$

Each individual test has level $\alpha$, meaning each has probability at most $\alpha$ of rejecting its null when that null is true. But what is the probability of making at least one false rejection across all $m$ tests?

Definition — Familywise Error Rate (FWER)

The FWER is the probability of making at least one Type I error (false rejection) across the entire family of $m$ tests:

$$ \text{FWER} = P(\text{at least one false rejection}). $$

4.2 A Toy Example That Says It All

Example — two independent tests at level $\alpha$

Suppose we observe two independent samples: $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1)$ and $Y_1, \dots, Y_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, 1)$, and we run two independent tests, each at level $\alpha$:

  • Test #1: $H_0^{(1)}: \theta = 0$ vs $H_1^{(1)}: \theta \ne 0$.
  • Test #2: $H_0^{(2)}: \mu = 0$ vs $H_1^{(2)}: \mu \ne 0$.

If both nulls are true, then

$$ P(\text{at least one rejection}) = 1 - P(\text{neither rejects}) = 1 - (1-\alpha)^2. $$

For $\alpha = 0.05$ this gives $1 - 0.95^2 \approx 0.0975$ — almost double the intended level. And for $m$ independent tests at level $\alpha$, the FWER grows to $1 - (1-\alpha)^m$, which approaches $1$ rapidly:

With 100 tests at $\alpha = 0.05$, you're basically guaranteed at least one false rejection by chance alone.

Why this matters in practice

In genomics we often test $10{,}000+$ genes for association with a disease. In A/B testing we might run hundreds of experiments per quarter. In machine-learning benchmarks we compare many models on many datasets. Without correction, "statistically significant" findings are swamped by false positives. The garden-of-forking-paths problem in scientific research is a direct consequence of ignoring multiple testing.

4.3 The Bonferroni Correction

The fix is elegantly simple: test each hypothesis at level $\alpha/m$ instead of $\alpha$. The total FWER is then at most $\alpha$.

Definition — Bonferroni-corrected test

For $m$ testing problems, run each at level $\alpha/m$: use rejection regions $R^{(j)}_{\alpha/m}$ instead of $R^{(j)}_\alpha$. Equivalently, compare each p-value $p_j$ against the threshold $\alpha/m$, or multiply each p-value by $m$ and compare to $\alpha$.

The correctness relies on a single elementary inequality:

Tool — Union Bound

For any events $A_1, \dots, A_m$:

$$ P\!\left( \bigcup_{j=1}^{m} A_j \right) \le \sum_{j=1}^{m} P(A_j). $$

This holds without any independence assumption and is a consequence of sigma-additivity for disjoint sets combined with inclusion-exclusion.

Why Bonferroni controls FWER

Let $J_0 \subseteq \{1, \dots, m\}$ be the set of indices for which $H_0^{(j)}$ is actually true, and let $A_j$ denote the event "test $j$ rejects." Then the FWER is $P\!\left(\bigcup_{j \in J_0} A_j\right)$, and

$$ \begin{aligned} P\!\left( \bigcup_{j \in J_0} A_j \right) &\le \sum_{j \in J_0} P(A_j) && \text{(union bound)} \\ &\le \sum_{j \in J_0} \sup_{\theta \in \Theta_0^{(j)}} P_\theta(A_j) && \\ &\le |J_0| \cdot \frac{\alpha}{m} && \text{(each test at level } \alpha/m\text{)} \\ &\le m \cdot \frac{\alpha}{m} = \alpha. \end{aligned} $$

No independence assumption was needed — the union bound works for any joint distribution of the tests. This is Bonferroni's main strength.

Example — Bonferroni in practice

Suppose a pharmaceutical company is testing a new drug on $m = 20$ different biomarkers, each with its own hypothesis test. They want an overall FWER of $\alpha = 0.05$.

  • Without correction: reject $H_0^{(j)}$ when $p_j < 0.05$. The probability of at least one false positive could be close to $1 - 0.95^{20} \approx 0.64$. Unacceptable.
  • With Bonferroni: reject $H_0^{(j)}$ only when $p_j < 0.05 / 20 = 0.0025$. Equivalently, compute "adjusted p-values" $\tilde p_j = \min(1, 20 \cdot p_j)$ and reject when $\tilde p_j < 0.05$.

So a biomarker with $p = 0.01$ — which would have seemed significant on its own — is not rejected after correction, because it doesn't survive the stricter threshold that accounts for having looked at 20 things.

Intuition — why divide by $m$?

If you give yourself $m$ chances to find a false positive, each at probability $\alpha$, you get roughly $m\alpha$ total error (by the union bound, up to overlap). To keep the total at $\alpha$, shrink each chance to $\alpha/m$. The union bound is loose when the tests are positively correlated, so Bonferroni is conservative — it controls FWER at or below $\alpha$, and often strictly below.

Trade-off — power loss

Bonferroni's simplicity comes at a cost: testing at level $\alpha/m$ means individual tests lose statistical power. For very large $m$ (say, $m = 10^4$ genes), the threshold $\alpha/m = 5 \times 10^{-6}$ is so strict that only very strong effects get discovered. This motivates more sophisticated procedures — Holm's step-down method, or the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure which controls the False Discovery Rate instead of FWER — but those are stories for another lecture.

Key Takeaways

  • Independence testing generalises to $I \times J$ tables. Pearson's $\chi^2$ statistic measures the discrepancy between observed cell counts and the counts expected under independence (row marginal × column marginal / $n$), and follows $\chi^2_{(I-1)(J-1)}$ asymptotically under $H_0$.
  • Degrees of freedom $= \dim(\Theta) - \dim(\Theta_0)$. The $(I-1)(J-1)$ comes from a clean dimension-counting argument using the probability simplex — $IJ - 1$ parameters in the full model minus $(I-1) + (J-1)$ under independence.
  • The likelihood ratio test gives an equivalent asymptotic story. Comparing the best unconstrained fit to the best independence-constrained fit yields $\lambda_n$, which converges in distribution to the same $\chi^2_{(I-1)(J-1)}$ under $H_0$. Pearson and LRT are two views of the same underlying likelihood geometry.
  • p-values are a continuous summary of evidence. The p-value is the smallest level at which a test rejects given the data, equivalently the probability of seeing a test statistic at least as extreme as observed under (the worst-case) $H_0$. They refine the binary reject/don't-reject verdict by showing how far the data lies from the null.
  • Running many tests inflates the familywise error rate. Even $m = 10$ independent tests at $\alpha = 0.05$ produce FWER $\approx 40\%$. At $m = 100$ it's nearly $1$. A level-$\alpha$ guarantee is a promise about one test, not many.
  • The Bonferroni correction is a distribution-free fix. Testing each hypothesis at level $\alpha/m$ controls FWER at $\alpha$ via the union bound — no independence assumption required. It's conservative (and can lose power for large $m$), but its simplicity and robustness make it the standard baseline for multiple-testing correction.
