Lecture 5 — Hypothesis Testing: Foundations

Course: Core Concepts in Statistical Learning Lecture 5 Date: 2026-04-13

This lecture introduces hypothesis testing, the third pillar of classical statistical inference (after point estimation and confidence sets). We develop the vocabulary — null and alternative hypotheses, Type I and Type II errors, level, power — and then build our first family of asymptotic tests: the Wald tests. The guiding idea throughout: a statistical test is a decision rule that lets the data speak, while carefully controlling how often we are fooled.

1. The Core Question: When Do Numbers Disagree "Enough"?

Imagine you are comparing two machine-learning models on the same test set. Model A misclassifies 82 rows; model B misclassifies 76. Is B actually better, or is the gap just random wiggle? Hypothesis testing is the formal answer to "is this difference real or a coincidence?"

Motivating Example — Two Prediction Algorithms

Let $p_i = \mathbb{P}(\text{algorithm } i \text{ makes a wrong prediction})$ for $i \in \{1, 2\}$.

We want to decide between:

  • $p_1 = p_2$: the two algorithms are equally accurate.
  • $p_1 \ne p_2$: one is genuinely more accurate.

Even if $p_1 = p_2$ in truth, sample error rates will almost never be exactly equal. The question is whether the observed gap is larger than random fluctuation would reasonably produce.

2. The Vocabulary of Testing

We observe a random sample $X \sim f(\cdot \,;\, \theta)$ where $\theta$ is unknown. We split the parameter space $\Theta$ into two disjoint regions $\Theta_0$ and $\Theta_1$ (with $\Theta_0 \cap \Theta_1 = \emptyset$), and we want to test:

$$H_0 : \theta \in \Theta_0 \quad \text{versus} \quad H_1 : \theta \in \Theta_1.$$
Null and Alternative Hypotheses
  • $H_0$ (null hypothesis): the default, conservative, or "status-quo" claim. Typically something like "no effect" or "no difference".
  • $H_1$ (alternative hypothesis): the claim we would need evidence to support.

Based on the data, we make one of two decisions:

  • Reject $H_0$ — the data provide enough evidence to favor $H_1$.
  • Do not reject $H_0$ — the data do not provide enough evidence against $H_0$. (Note: this is not the same as "accepting $H_0$ as true" — absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.)

2.1 The Two Kinds of Mistakes

Every test can go wrong in exactly two ways:

Decision \ Truth $H_0$ is true $H_1$ is true
Reject $H_0$ Type I error (false alarm) ✓ Correct
Do not reject $H_0$ ✓ Correct Type II error (missed signal)
Intuition — Think of a Smoke Detector

$H_0$ = "no fire". A Type I error is the detector shrieking while you are calmly making toast (false alarm). A Type II error is the detector staying silent while the kitchen burns (missed signal).

You cannot drive both to zero at once: a hyper-sensitive detector has fewer misses but more false alarms; a sleepy one has fewer false alarms but misses real fires. The whole craft of testing lies in managing this tradeoff.

2.2 Rejection Region, Level, and Power

Rejection Region

The rejection region is the set of data outcomes that lead us to reject $H_0$:

$$R = \{x \in \mathcal{X} : \text{the test rejects } H_0 \text{ when } X = x\}.$$

A test is simply a function $f : \mathcal{X} \to \{0, 1\}$ with $f(x) = \mathbf{1}_{\{x \in R\}}$ (1 = reject, 0 = do not reject).

Level and Size
  • For $\theta \in \Theta_0$, the probability $\mathbb{P}_\theta(X \in R)$ is the Type I error probability at $\theta$.
  • The level of the test is the worst-case Type I error: $\sup_{\theta \in \Theta_0} \mathbb{P}_\theta(X \in R)$.
  • We say the test has level $\alpha$ if $\sup_{\theta \in \Theta_0} \mathbb{P}_\theta(X \in R) \le \alpha$.
  • It has size $\alpha$ if equality holds.
Power

For $\theta \in \Theta_1$, the power at $\theta$ is

$$\beta(\theta) = \mathbb{P}_\theta(X \in R).$$

This is the probability of correctly rejecting $H_0$ when the alternative is true. The Type II error probability at $\theta$ is $1 - \beta(\theta)$.

Intuition. The level is a promise to the skeptic: "If $H_0$ really holds, I will wrongly reject it at most $\alpha$ fraction of the time." Typical choices are $\alpha = 0.05$ or $\alpha = 0.01$. Power is a measure of detection ability — higher is better. We fix level first (to control false alarms), then try to maximize power (to catch real effects).

2.3 Simple vs. Composite Hypotheses

  • A hypothesis is simple if it specifies a single distribution (i.e., $|\Theta_0| = 1$ or $|\Theta_1| = 1$).
  • Otherwise it is composite.
Example — Three flavors of test
  • $H_0: \theta = \theta_0$ vs. $H_1: \theta = \theta_1$   — simple vs. simple.
  • $H_0: \theta \le \theta_0$ vs. $H_1: \theta > \theta_0$   — composite vs. composite (one-sided).
  • $H_0: \theta = \theta_0$ vs. $H_1: \theta \ne \theta_0$   — simple vs. composite (two-sided).

3. The Paradigm: Control Type I, Then Maximize Power

Classical testing follows a strict two-step logic. Think of it like a courtroom: we presume the defendant ($H_0$) innocent. We never convict on a whim — first, we set a strict bar that makes wrongful convictions rare ($\alpha$). Only then do we try to make our method as good as possible at convicting the truly guilty (power).

  1. Cap the Type I error at a chosen level $\alpha$: require $\sup_{\theta \in \Theta_0} \mathbb{P}_\theta(X \in R) \le \alpha$.
  2. Maximize power among all tests satisfying (1).
Uniformly Most Powerful (UMP) Test

A level-$\alpha$ test with power function $\beta(\cdot)$ is Uniformly Most Powerful if, for every other level-$\alpha$ test with power $\beta'(\cdot)$:

$$\beta'(\theta) \le \beta(\theta) \quad \text{for all } \theta \in \Theta_1.$$

"Uniformly" means dominant at every single $\theta$ in the alternative — not just on average.

UMP tests are the gold standard, but they do not always exist (especially for two-sided alternatives). When they do, we are in a very fortunate spot.

4. Worked Example: A Test for a Normal Mean

Let $X_1, \dots, X_n \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1)$ with $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$. Test

$$H_0 : \theta = 0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1 : \theta > 0,$$

at level $\alpha \in (0, 1)$.

4.1 Building the Test

Intuitively, if $\theta > 0$, the sample mean $\bar{X}_n$ should tend to be large. So reject $H_0$ when $\bar{X}_n$ exceeds some threshold $c$:

$$\text{Reject } H_0 \iff \bar{X}_n > c.$$

We choose $c$ so that the test has size $\alpha$, i.e., $\mathbb{P}_{\theta=0}(\bar{X}_n > c) = \alpha$.

Under $H_0$, $\bar{X}_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1/n)$, so $\sqrt{n}\,\bar{X}_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. Hence:

$$\mathbb{P}_{\theta=0}(\bar{X}_n > c) = \mathbb{P}(Z > \sqrt{n}\, c) = \alpha,$$

which gives $\sqrt{n}\, c = z_{1-\alpha}$, the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. Therefore:

$$c = \frac{z_{1-\alpha}}{\sqrt{n}}, \qquad R = \left\{ (x_1, \dots, x_n) : \bar{x}_n \ge \frac{z_{1-\alpha}}{\sqrt{n}} \right\}.$$

4.2 The Power Function

How good is this test at detecting a truly positive $\theta$? For $\theta > 0$:

$$ \begin{aligned} \beta(\theta) &= \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \bar{X}_n > \frac{z_{1-\alpha}}{\sqrt{n}} \right) \\ &= \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta) > z_{1-\alpha} - \sqrt{n}\, \theta \right) \\ &= \mathbb{P}(Z > z_{1-\alpha} - \sqrt{n}\, \theta) \\ &= 1 - F_Z(z_{1-\alpha} - \sqrt{n}\, \theta), \end{aligned} $$

where $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ and $F_Z$ its CDF.

Two crucial observations
  • For fixed $n$: the power $\beta(\theta)$ grows as $\theta$ moves farther above 0. Larger effects are easier to detect — obvious and reassuring.
  • For fixed $\theta > 0$: as $n \to \infty$, $\sqrt{n}\,\theta \to \infty$, so $\beta(\theta) \to 1$. More data ⟹ near-certain detection. This is the mathematical soul of "with enough data, any real effect becomes detectable."

5. Asymptotic Tests and the Wald Test

In realistic problems (unknown variance, unknown distribution shape), constructing an exact level-$\alpha$ test is often impossible. We fall back on tests whose level holds in the limit as $n \to \infty$: asymptotic tests. The Wald test is the workhorse of this category.

5.1 Setup

Observe $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim f(\cdot\,;\, \theta)$ with $\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. Test

$$H_0 : \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1 : \theta \ne \theta_0.$$

Suppose we have an asymptotically normal estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ of $\theta$. Under $H_0$:

$$W_n := \frac{\hat{\theta}_n - \theta_0}{\widehat{\mathrm{se}}(\hat{\theta}_n)} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1).$$

where $\widehat{\mathrm{se}}(\hat{\theta}_n)$ is a consistent estimator of the standard error.

The Wald Test (two-sided, asymptotic level $\alpha$)
$$\text{Reject } H_0 \iff |W_n| > z_{1-\alpha/2}.$$

Equivalently: reject when the estimator is more than $z_{1-\alpha/2}$ standard errors away from the hypothesized value.

Intuition. $W_n$ measures, in standard-error units, how far the data's best guess $\hat{\theta}_n$ lands from the hypothesized $\theta_0$. If $H_0$ holds, $W_n$ behaves like a standard normal — so $|W_n|$ exceeding ~1.96 (at $\alpha = 0.05$) would happen only 5% of the time by chance. When it does exceed the threshold, we take it as evidence against $H_0$.

5.2 Verifying the Asymptotic Level

The probability of a false rejection under $H_0$ is:

$$ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{P}_{\theta_0}(|W_n| > z_{1-\alpha/2}) &= 1 - \mathbb{P}_{\theta_0}(-z_{1-\alpha/2} \le W_n \le z_{1-\alpha/2}) \\ &\xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 1 - \bigl[F_Z(z_{1-\alpha/2}) - F_Z(-z_{1-\alpha/2})\bigr] \\ &= 1 - \bigl[(1 - \tfrac{\alpha}{2}) - \tfrac{\alpha}{2}\bigr] = \alpha. \end{aligned} $$

The symmetry of the standard normal around 0 is what lets the two tails combine cleanly into $\alpha$.

6. Wald Test for a Mean (Unknown Variance)

This is the most commonly applied Wald test. Let $X_1, \dots, X_n$ be i.i.d. with unknown mean $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$ and unknown variance $\sigma^2 \in (0, \infty)$. Test

$$H_0 : \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1 : \theta \ne \theta_0.$$

6.1 Two Ingredients

(i) By the CLT:

$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta)}{\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1).$$

But $\sigma$ is unknown — so we estimate it. Let $S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2$ be the sample variance.

(ii) We need $S_n \xrightarrow{P} \sigma$. Here is the quick derivation:

$$ \begin{aligned} S_n^2 &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \theta)^2 - (\bar{X}_n - \theta)^2. \end{aligned} $$

By the WLLN, $\frac{1}{n}\sum (X_i - \theta)^2 \xrightarrow{P} \mathbb{E}[(X-\theta)^2] = \sigma^2$. Also $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \theta$, so by the continuous mapping theorem $(\bar{X}_n - \theta)^2 \xrightarrow{P} 0$. Therefore $S_n^2 \xrightarrow{P} \sigma^2$, and again by continuous mapping, $S_n \xrightarrow{P} \sigma$.

6.2 Assembling the Test via Slutsky

By Slutsky's theorem (a random sequence converging in distribution times a random sequence converging in probability to a constant):

$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta)}{S_n} = \underbrace{\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta)}{\sigma}}_{\xrightarrow{d}\, \mathcal{N}(0,1)} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\sigma}{S_n}}_{\xrightarrow{P}\, 1} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1).$$
Wald Test for a Mean

Reject $H_0: \theta = \theta_0$ at asymptotic level $\alpha$ if and only if

$$\left| \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta_0)}{S_n} \right| > z_{1-\alpha/2}.$$
Connection to the t-test

If you have seen the classical one-sample t-test, you will recognize this statistic. The difference: the t-test uses a $t$-distribution threshold (exact under normality); the Wald test uses a normal threshold (asymptotic, distribution-free). For large $n$ they agree, because $t_{n-1}$ converges to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

7. Application: Comparing Two Prediction Algorithms

We now return to the opening motivation and solve it with the Wald machinery.

7.1 Setup

For each of $n$ test rows, record:

$$X_i = \mathbf{1}_{\{\text{algo 1 wrong on row } i\}}, \qquad Y_i = \mathbf{1}_{\{\text{algo 2 wrong on row } i\}}.$$

Assume the pairs $(X_1, Y_1), \dots, (X_n, Y_n)$ are i.i.d. — reasonable because the test rows themselves are independent. Crucially, we do not assume $X_i$ and $Y_i$ are independent of each other; on the same row, both algorithms see the same input, so their errors are naturally correlated. The "paired" design handles this.

Test

$$H_0: p_1 = p_2 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: p_1 \ne p_2.$$

7.2 The Paired-Differences Trick

Define the per-row differences $D_i = X_i - Y_i \in \{-1, 0, 1\}$. Then $D_1, \dots, D_n$ are i.i.d. with

$$\mathbb{E}(D_1) = p_1 - p_2.$$

So the original question — "is $p_1 = p_2$?" — becomes "is the mean of the $D_i$'s zero?" That is exactly the single-mean Wald setup.

Paired Wald Test (asymptotic level $\alpha$)

Reject $H_0: p_1 = p_2$ if and only if

$$\frac{\sqrt{n}\,|\bar{X}_n - \bar{Y}_n|}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - Y_i - (\bar{X}_n - \bar{Y}_n))^2}} > z_{1-\alpha/2}.$$
Numerical sketch

Suppose $n = 500$, algo 1 misclassified 82 rows, algo 2 misclassified 76, and they disagreed on 18 rows. Then $\bar{X}_n - \bar{Y}_n = (82 - 76)/500 = 0.012$. The denominator (sample SD of the $D_i$'s) is small because the algorithms agree on most rows — say around $0.19$. The Wald statistic is roughly

$$\frac{\sqrt{500} \cdot 0.012}{0.19} \approx 1.41.$$

At $\alpha = 0.05$, $z_{0.975} \approx 1.96$. Since $1.41 < 1.96$, we do not reject $H_0$ — the 6-row difference is consistent with random noise on this test set.

Why pairing matters

Had we treated $X$'s and $Y$'s as two independent samples, the denominator would instead estimate $\mathrm{Var}(X) + \mathrm{Var}(Y)$ — ignoring their positive correlation. This makes the test much less powerful: real differences between correlated algorithms routinely fail to be detected by the unpaired test. The paired test exploits the per-row common structure, which is why the sum $\sum (X_i - Y_i - (\bar{X}_n - \bar{Y}_n))^2$ appears — it estimates $\mathrm{Var}(D)$ directly and benefits from cancellation.

8. Connection to Confidence Intervals

Tests and confidence intervals are two sides of the same coin. Given the Wald statistic construction, the set of values of $\theta_0$ for which we would not reject $H_0 : \theta = \theta_0$ at level $\alpha$ is exactly the $(1-\alpha)$ confidence interval:

$$C_n = \left[\hat{\theta}_n - z_{1-\alpha/2} \cdot \widehat{\mathrm{se}}(\hat{\theta}_n), \;\; \hat{\theta}_n + z_{1-\alpha/2} \cdot \widehat{\mathrm{se}}(\hat{\theta}_n)\right].$$

This is the "duality" between tests and intervals: the Wald CI collects all null values that the data do not clearly rule out at level $\alpha$. If $\theta_0 \notin C_n$, we reject; if $\theta_0 \in C_n$, we do not. Seen this way, the asymptotic confidence interval for the Bernoulli parameter constructed in Lecture 3 is already implicitly a family of Wald tests.

9. Key Takeaways

  • Testing vocabulary. A test splits $\Theta$ into $\Theta_0$ and $\Theta_1$; a rejection region encodes the decision rule. Level bounds the Type I error probability; power measures how often real effects are detected.
  • Asymmetric treatment. We prioritize controlling Type I error (false alarms) to a chosen $\alpha$, then try to maximize power under that constraint. UMP tests are the ideal when they exist.
  • The Wald recipe. Any asymptotically normal estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ with a consistent standard error gives a Wald test: reject when $|\hat{\theta}_n - \theta_0| / \widehat{\mathrm{se}}$ exceeds $z_{1-\alpha/2}$.
  • Slutsky is the glue. Plugging in a consistent variance estimator like $S_n^2$ preserves the limiting normal distribution — this is what makes Wald tests usable in practice.
  • Pairing exploits dependence. For comparing two algorithms on the same test set, transforming to per-row differences $D_i$ reduces the problem to a one-sample mean test and correctly accounts for correlation.
  • Duality. A two-sided Wald test at level $\alpha$ and the corresponding $(1-\alpha)$ Wald confidence interval are the same object, read from different sides.
